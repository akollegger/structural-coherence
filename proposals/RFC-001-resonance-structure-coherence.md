## Abstract

We describe what appears to be a latent feature of growing information corpora: a regularity relating the signal a corpus carries, the inconsistency it accumulates, and the structure that mediates between them — present implicitly in any body of information with relational regularity, whether or not it has been made explicit. Starting from the premise that information is signal and inconsistency is noise, we observe that inconsistencies compound as volume grows, while unaided interpretive dampening scales at best linearly. We introduce a coherence ratio and show that maintaining it as volume grows requires *structural leverage* to grow at least as fast as $V^{\beta}$, where the composite exponent $\beta = 1 + \delta - \gamma$ pits the growth of inconsistency (governed by $\delta$, how far real conflict departs from uniform pairwise interaction) against the growth of usable signal (governed by $\gamma$, how far enrichment departs from linear) — so that the sign of $\beta$ alone separates a regime where more data must be paid for with more structure from one where more data helps for free. We decouple structure's two effects — enrichment (signal generation) and dampening (conflict suppression) — into independent functions, and show that the functional class of each, together with the composite exponent $\beta$, determines whether coherence is sustainable and at what structural cost. We use this decomposition to classify kinds of structure, from controlled vocabularies to type systems to graphs, and to locate a continuum running from unstructured prose to relational and graph-structured data; we are explicit that the compounding often claimed for graphs holds for graph-plus-enforced-schema rather than for unconstrained instance data alone. We offer the account as a descriptive lens for information architecture rather than an empirically validated theory, and we outline an experimental program, including the operationalization of inconsistency itself, to test its central predictions. We emphasize throughout that the framework reframes a classical set of engineering tradeoffs — compute, storage, freshness, and write-time coordination against the read-time optimization that structure enables — as a decision about where reasoning work should live, and shows that structure is what lets a system choose.

## 1. Introduction

Large information systems tend to follow a recognizable trajectory. A retrieval system, a database, a codebase, or a search index works well at modest scale, degrades as it grows, and regains coherence once some organizing structure appears — an index, a re-ranker, a type system, a knowledge graph. These are usually understood as independent engineering solutions to independent problems. What is striking, and what motivates this paper, is that the same shape of solution keeps reappearing: teams that share no code, no domain, and no vocabulary independently arrive at structurally similar answers, and often at answers their disciplines had already named long before. The recurrence is the clue. When a regularity is discovered over and over by people who were not looking for the same thing, it is usually because the regularity was there to be found rather than invented each time.

This paper proceeds from that stance. We do not propose that structure be imposed on information to rescue it; we observe that coherence-preserving structure appears to be a *latent* feature of any body of information with relational regularity — present implicitly whether or not anyone has made it explicit — and we set out to describe that feature rather than to prescribe it. The relationship we describe is between three quantities: the signal a corpus carries, the inconsistency it accumulates, and the structure that mediates between them as the corpus grows. Our central object, the coherence ratio, is a measurement of a phenomenon we take to be already at work in the corpus, not a mechanism we are adding to it. In this we intend the formal apparatus in the spirit of any descriptive law: an attempt to state explicitly, and provisionally, a regularity that operates implicitly and would operate whether or not we had written it down. That the specific functional forms are our first approximations rather than derived truths is, on this reading, exactly what one expects of a description reaching for a phenomenon it has not yet fully resolved.

We make three contributions. First, we give a formal bound relating structural leverage to corpus volume — a description of how much structure a growing corpus must already carry, or come to carry, to remain coherent (Section 3). Second, we decouple the two mechanisms by which structure acts — enrichment and dampening — and show that treating them as independent functions clarifies which kinds of structure sustain coherence and which cannot (Sections 4 and 5). Third, we position a wide range of information representations, from prose to graphs, on a single continuum, with running parallels to the type systems of programming languages (Section 6). We describe the experiments that would test the framework's central predictions (Section 8).

### 1.1 Scope and stance

We define coherence narrowly, as the relative absence of internal contradiction. This is deliberately not a theory of information *quality*: a corpus may be perfectly coherent yet stale, incomplete, or irrelevant. Coherence in our sense is necessary but not sufficient for usefulness. We adopt this narrow definition because it is the aspect of information most directly and predictably affected by structure, and because it admits formal treatment. The relationship between this internal notion and the external ones it sets aside — correspondence with observable reality, and consensus among distinct actors — is a natural extension we take up as future work (Section 10) rather than a gap; it is deferred, not overlooked.

One clarification concerns the stance itself, and it is the paper's central ontological commitment. We take internal coherence to be an **intrinsic** property of a corpus — a matter of whether its statements are in fact mutually consistent — rather than a matter of conformance to any externally declared structure. This distinguishes the account from a strand of the knowledge-representation tradition in which conformance to a declared ontology, schema, or set of shapes *is* the definition of consistency. On our reading, conformance is not the arbiter of coherence but a fallible **epistemic probe** of it: an external structure $S$ is an instrument for surfacing incoherence that is, or is not, already latent in the corpus, and different structures are different, individually imperfect instruments rather than competing definitions. The prose-to-graph continuum of Section 6 is, in this light, a ladder of increasingly formal probes, and the coherence they make legible is a property the informal end of the ladder already carries — which is why a reader registers contradiction in unstructured prose with no schema at all. We name two consequences and deliberately develop neither here. First, a probe can be *unfaithful*: an ill-fitting or itself-incoherent $S$ can manufacture apparent conflict, and a too-weak $S$ can miss real conflict, so a single probe's verdict is not self-certifying. Distinguishing incoherence latent in the corpus from an artifact of the probe requires agreement across independent probes — an estimation problem we flag and defer to future work (Section 10) rather than solve in this draft. Second, this reframes an older question about whether coherence-preserving structure is *discovered* or *imposed*. The motivating cases (authority latent in a link graph, types latent in the shapes values take, clusters latent in topology) favor the descriptive, latent reading but do not compel it; an adversarial reading, on which these structures were *chosen* and their latency is partly post-hoc rationalization, remains coherent, and we do not claim to defeat it. Our reasons for the latent stance — that the same structures are independently rediscovered across designers and domains, and often coincide with regularities domains had named before any system was built (Section 4.3) — favor it without settling it. A reader who prefers the imposition reading can take the framework as a description of *which impositions succeed*, since the formal results are indifferent to whether the structure they describe was found or made; what the intrinsic-coherence commitment fixes is only that structure is a probe of consistency, not its definition.

We also stress that the formal apparatus below is intended to describe an implicit phenomenon, not to legislate one. The functional forms we posit are illustrative first approximations, and we treat their provisionality as proper to a descriptive account rather than as a defect: a law that states a regularity explicitly is answerable to the regularity, and may be refined or replaced without the underlying phenomenon being any less real. The value of the framework is in the questions it lets practitioners ask and in what it helps them see, not in the specific curves it draws.

## 2. Motivating Observations

Before formalizing, we ground the argument in cases that will be familiar to practitioners. Each follows the same three-part pattern: a system operates coherently at low volume, loses coherence as volume grows, and regains it once structure that was implicit in the data is made explicit and usable. We describe these as interventions for brevity, but note that in each the "new" structure largely makes accessible a regularity the data already contained — authority latent in a link graph, types latent in the shapes values already took, relationships latent in co-occurrence.

**Vector retrieval.** Similarity search over embeddings finds relevant material reliably when the corpus is small. As the corpus grows, the number of items exceeding any fixed similarity threshold grows faster than the number that are genuinely relevant, and precision degrades. The standard remedies — re-ranking, metadata prefiltering, hybrid lexical-semantic-structural search — are all impositions of structure that restore an ordering similarity alone can no longer provide.

**Web search.** Early keyword-frequency ranking sufficed for a small web. As the web grew, adversarial and incidental overlap saturated the relevance signal. PageRank restored coherence by adding a graph layer encoding authority; subsequent structural layers (entity recognition, knowledge graphs, semantic signals) extended the same strategy.

**Code at scale.** A short program maintains coherence in the author's working memory. As a codebase and its contributor set grow, implicit assumptions conflict and behavior becomes unpredictable. Type systems, module boundaries, and interface contracts are structural mechanisms introduced specifically to restore coherence that informal discipline can no longer hold.

In each case the intervention is rarely named as "adding structure," and is rarely recognized as an instance of a general pattern. A central aim of this paper is to make that pattern explicit.

## 3. A Coherence Bound

### 3.1 Definitions

Let $V$ denote corpus volume, measured as the number of atomic statements or facts. Let:

- $I(V)$ be the *information* — the coherent, usable signal the corpus carries.
- $\epsilon(V)$ be the *inconsistency* — the count of contradictions, latent or manifest.
- $S$ be a scalar summary of the corpus's *structural complexity* (schema richness, constraint count, relational density).

Define the **coherence ratio**:

$$\mathcal{C}(V) = \frac{I(V)}{\epsilon(V)}.$$

A note on dimension is needed before this ratio can carry weight, because $I$ and $\epsilon$ are not obviously commensurable: $I$ is a quantity of usable signal and $\epsilon$ is a count of contradictions, and their raw ratio has no natural unit. We make the ratio well-defined by reading both quantities as *densities relative to volume* — $I(V)$ as usable signal per unit volume and $\epsilon(V)$ as contradictions per unit volume, each normalized against the same $V$ — so that $\mathcal{C}$ is a dimensionless ratio of two per-statement rates rather than a quotient of unlike quantities. Equivalently, one may fix any consistent unit for signal and measure $\epsilon$ in the same unit by assigning each contradiction a signal-equivalent cost; the choice of normalization affects the constant $\mathcal{C}_{\min}$ below but not the exponents that are the paper's actual subject. Throughout, it is the *scaling* of $\mathcal{C}$ with $V$, not its absolute value, that carries the argument.

We are interested in the conditions under which $\mathcal{C}(V)$ does not tend to zero as $V \to \infty$.

#### 3.1.1 Operationalizing $\epsilon$: a necessary caveat

Before proceeding we must be explicit about a difficulty the notation otherwise hides. $V$ is a clean count of atomic statements. $\epsilon$ is not a comparably clean count of atomic conflicts. A single contradiction between two statements is an unambiguous unit, but real corpora exhibit *cascading* inconsistency: if statement $C$ is derived from or depends on contradicted statement $B$, it is not obvious whether the corpus contains one inconsistency, two, or a chain whose length should itself be counted. We do not resolve this here. For the remainder of the paper, $\epsilon(V)$ should be read as *a* reasonable operationalization — for instance, the count of directly contradictory statement pairs, before propagation — rather than *the* unique or correct one. Section 8.1 revisits this as an open measurement problem that the experimental program must settle before the bound below can be tested rather than merely illustrated. Everything that follows inherits this caveat: the bound is a relationship between whatever operational $\epsilon$ one adopts and the volume that produced it, not a claim that inconsistency has a single natural unit.

A further distinction cuts across the counting problem: inconsistency comes in *hard* and *soft* varieties, and the framework applies to both but must not silently conflate them. A **hard** inconsistency is a binary violation — a type error, a breached uniqueness constraint, a pair of statements that cannot both be true under the schema's logic. A **soft** inconsistency is a probabilistic anomaly: statements that are individually valid and jointly permissible yet in tension. That a person `lives in Seattle` and `works in New York` violates no schema and entails no contradiction, yet it is anomalous in a way a well-calibrated reader or model registers as worth noting. This is the conformant-yet-incoherent case the intrinsic-coherence stance of Section 1.1 anticipates: the pair passes every formal conformance probe yet is intrinsically in tension, so an $\epsilon$ defined only over conformance violations cannot see it. Hard inconsistency is the natural currency of a priori, type-based dampening (Section 4.1), which rejects what it can prove invalid; soft inconsistency is the natural currency of a posteriori, shape-based dampening (Sections 4.2–4.3), which flags what is statistically unusual without proving it wrong. This matters especially for LLM-driven corpora, where most real incoherence is soft — tension, drift, and implausibility rather than formal contradiction — and where an $\epsilon$ defined only over hard violations would miss the dominant failure mode. The bound is agnostic between the two, but any empirical instantiation must state which it is counting; the two do not grow at the same rate, respond to the same dampening, or carry the same cost when left unaddressed.

This has a consequence for the bound derived below that we make explicit rather than leave implied: because hard and soft inconsistency grow at different rates, the conflict exponent $\delta$ — and therefore the composite $\beta$ — is *indexed by inconsistency type*. A corpus has, in general, a $\beta_{\text{hard}}$ and a $\beta_{\text{soft}}$, and they need not share a sign. A system can satisfy the bound on hard inconsistency (no logical contradictions accumulate faster than structure can suppress them) while failing it on soft inconsistency (semantic tension compounds unchecked), or the reverse. The single $\beta$ carried through the derivation below should therefore be read as "the $\beta$ for whichever inconsistency type is being measured," not as a single corpus-wide constant; the derivation is identical in form for each type, but the exponents, and the practical conclusions, may differ between them. For LLM-driven systems the operative quantity is $\beta_{\text{soft}}$, which is both the harder to measure and the more consequential — a point the experimental program (Section 8.1) must confront directly rather than defining $\epsilon$ over hard violations because they are easier to count.

### 3.2 The compounding of inconsistency

Absent structure, any two statements in a corpus may in principle conflict. If conflicts were uniformly likely across all pairs, the number of pairs — and thus an upper bound on potential pairwise inconsistencies — would grow as

$$\epsilon_0(V) \sim \binom{V}{2} \sim \frac{V^2}{2}.$$

We use this uniform-pairwise model as a **worst-case reference bound**, not as an empirical claim about real corpora, and we flag its limits immediately rather than deferring them. Facts do not conflict uniformly at random: information clusters by domain, schema, and entity boundary, and a statement about a user's billing address has no realistic prospect of contradicting a statement about a server's temperature. If conflicts are local to clusters of size roughly $k \ll V$, the effective growth of $\epsilon_0$ is closer to $V \cdot k$ — linear in $V$ — or, allowing cluster size itself to grow slowly with corpus size, some intermediate order such as $V^{1+\delta}$ for small $\delta \geq 0$, or $V\log V$. We therefore write the general form

$$\epsilon_0(V) \sim V^{1+\delta}, \qquad 0 \leq \delta \leq 1,$$

with $\delta = 1$ recovering the fully-connected worst case above and $\delta \to 0$ the fully-clustered, near-linear case. The true value of $\delta$ for a given domain is an empirical question — one we take up directly in Section 8.3 — and the two endpoints bracket a real uncertainty rather than a rhetorical one. We carry $\delta$ through the derivation below so that the resulting bound is explicit about which regime it describes, rather than silently assuming the pessimistic one. We treat $\delta$ here as a property of the domain to be discovered, but it need not be left to chance: Section 4.2 describes a structural mechanism, topological community detection, that actively produces a favorable $\delta$ by making conflict-checking local to a detected cluster rather than global to the corpus — a technique for engineering the exponent down rather than merely measuring it.

### 3.3 Structure's two effects

Structure acts on this picture in two distinct ways. It *suppresses conflict* by constraining what statements can coexist, and it *generates signal* by making inference possible. We model these with two independent functions:

$$\epsilon(V, S) = V^{1+\delta}\,\sigma(S), \qquad I(V, S) = V\,\phi(S),$$

where $\sigma(S) \in (0,1]$ is the **dampening factor** (decreasing in $S$: more structure, smaller conflict surface) and $\phi(S) \geq 1$ is the **enrichment factor** (increasing in $S$: more structure, more derivable signal).

The independence of $\sigma$ and $\phi$ is a central commitment of this paper and is developed in Section 4. We must also address, rather than merely flag, a second dimension of the model: the linear form $I(V,S) = V\phi(S)$ treats enrichment as uniform across the corpus, whereas real enrichment plausibly yields diminishing marginal returns — doubling a graph's size does not double the utility of every existing node, and realistic growth in usable signal more likely follows a sub-linear curve in $V$ once a corpus is large. We therefore generalize the enrichment term in the same way we generalized the conflict term, writing

$$I(V, S) = V^{\gamma}\,\phi(S), \qquad \gamma > 0,$$

where $\gamma = 1$ recovers the linear case, $\gamma < 1$ captures diminishing marginal enrichment, and $\gamma > 1$ captures *super-linear* enrichment — the regime in which each added statement makes disproportionately more signal derivable, as when graph traversal or compositional inference turns a linear addition of edges into combinatorially many derivable facts. We admit $\gamma > 1$ deliberately rather than by oversight. As the derivation in Section 3.4 makes explicit, the raw conflict term grows at least linearly (the exponent $1+\delta$ has a floor of $1$), so the benign regime in which coherence improves with volume at fixed structure — the $\beta < 0$ case that Section 3.4 and the abstract both claim — is reachable *only* if enrichment can outpace that floor, which requires $\gamma > 1$. Capping $\gamma$ at the linear case, as an earlier draft did, would silently foreclose a regime the derivation goes on to assert, forcing $\beta \geq 0$ for every admissible $\delta$; we lift the cap so the model can represent the "more data helps for free" case it already describes. This is not a cosmetic generalization. As the derivation in Section 3.4 shows, $\gamma$ enters the bound on equal footing with the clustering exponent $\delta$, and their *difference* rather than either alone governs whether coherence can be maintained — a point the linear simplification hides entirely, and one the review process rightly identified as changing the functional class of the required structural growth rather than merely its magnitude.

#### 3.3.1 A terminological clarification: enrichment versus inference

The paper uses "enrichment" and "inference" throughout, and the two are related closely enough to invite treating them as synonyms. They are not, and the distinction matters enough to fix before either term is used further.

**Enrichment** is the effect: it names $\phi(S)$ itself, the multiplier on usable signal. It is a claim about *quantity of derivable signal* and is agnostic about how that signal came to be derivable.

**Inference** is one mechanism that produces enrichment, not a synonym for it. Inference, as we use the term, denotes enrichment that is *reliably derivable from existing structure by a fixed rule*, without an independent, fallible synthesis step standing between the structure and the derived fact — a type resolving a generic instance, a subsumption relationship licensing a category membership, a traversal composing two relationships into a path. Not all enrichment is inferential in this sense. Embeddings, one of this paper's own enrichment-dominant examples in Section 4, raise $\phi$ through geometric induction — proximity in a learned space — which involves no rule-governed derivation step at all; there is nothing to point to that entails one document's relevance to another in the way a subsumption axiom entails a category membership. Embeddings are enrichment without inference in our sense, and the paper's use of "enrichment" for that example should be read literally, not as a loose stand-in for "inference."

This distinction bears directly on the compound quadrant's admissibility criterion, established in Section 4. What qualifies a mechanism as belonging inside the enrichment-without-fallible-synthesis family is not that it takes the specific form of classical logical entailment, but that it is produced by a fixed, rule-governed procedure applied to existing structure, with no independent model interposing a synthesis step of its own. This is the property that keeps an enrichment mechanism inside the compound quadrant rather than sliding into the generative, error-prone kind, and it covers more than inference proper — the next paragraph draws a further distinction within this family that Section 4.2 and 4.3 depend on. An LLM-generated summary of a community, by contrast, would be enrichment — it plausibly raises usable signal — without belonging to this family at all, because the summarizing model is exactly the kind of independent, fallible synthesis step the definition excludes; such a summary would need to be evaluated on its own terms, including for hallucination risk, rather than assumed to inherit any surrounding structure's dampening.

Within this rule-governed family, a further distinction is worth drawing precisely, because the paper uses two mechanisms that satisfy it in structurally different ways. **Inference proper** is *local*: a fixed rule applies to a small, bounded number of statements — typically a pair or triple — and produces one new statement, independent of corpus size. $A \to B$ and $B \to C$ entail $A \to C$ regardless of whether the corpus contains ten statements or ten billion; the rule does not reference the shape of the whole. **Motif detection**, developed in Section 4.3, is *global* in a specific sense: it recognizes a recurring, nameable configuration of relationships — a triangle, a feed-forward loop, a star — that is visible only by examining a neighborhood or the whole graph's topology, not by chaining pairwise rules. Critically, motif detection is not a reduce operation: unlike the community partitioning of Section 4.2, which collapses many nodes into one group, motif detection adds a labeled structural layer *on top of* the existing relationships without collapsing or summarizing any of them away. The triangle is a new, nameable entity, coexisting with the three edges that compose it, visible only at the level of the pattern rather than at the level of any single edge. We reserve "inference" for the local, pairwise case and use "motif detection" (Section 4.3) for this global, pattern-recognizing case; both are rule-governed and both stay inside the compound quadrant, but they differ in whether the rule is applied pointwise or extracted from the shape of a larger configuration, and Section 4.2's community detection is a third, reduce-shaped case distinct from both.

We therefore use "inference" narrowly throughout: the local, pairwise-rule subset of enrichment mechanisms that stay inside the compound quadrant, as distinguished from the global, pattern-recognizing subset (motif detection, Section 4.3) and the partitioning subset (community detection, Section 4.2). It is specifically the local, inferential subset that Section 7.3's discussion of accessible mutual information is about. That bridge concerns enrichment that surfaces mutual information already latent in stored structure via a local rule; it does not extend to enrichment produced by geometric induction, generative synthesis, motif detection, or community partitioning, each of which raises $\phi$ through a different means and would require separate treatment.

### 3.4 The bound

Substituting the generalized forms for both $\epsilon$ and $I$,

$$\mathcal{C}(V, S) = \frac{V^{\gamma}\,\phi(S)}{V^{1+\delta}\,\sigma(S)} = \frac{\phi(S)}{V^{1+\delta-\gamma}\,\sigma(S)} = \frac{\Lambda(S)}{V^{\,1+\delta-\gamma}},$$

where we define the **structural leverage**

$$\Lambda(S) = \frac{\phi(S)}{\sigma(S)}.$$

For coherence to be maintained at or above a floor $\mathcal{C}_{\min} > 0$, we require

$$\boxed{\;\Lambda(S(V)) \;\geq\; \mathcal{C}_{\min}\,V^{\,1+\delta-\gamma}\;}$$

where $\Lambda$ is implicitly a function of $V$ through the structure $S(V)$ maintained at that volume — it is the growth of $S$ with $V$, transmitted through $\Lambda$, that must satisfy the inequality.

The controlling quantity is the composite exponent $1 + \delta - \gamma$, and it is the interaction of the two generalizations — not either alone — that determines the regime. Writing $\beta = 1 + \delta - \gamma$:

- When $\beta > 0$ (conflict compounding outpaces enrichment's dilution of it), leverage must grow as $V^{\beta}$ and fixed structure cannot maintain coherence. The worst case $\delta = 1, \gamma = 1$ gives $\beta = 1$, recovering the linear-leverage requirement and matching the earlier $V^2/2$ derivation up to the constant folded into $\mathcal{C}_{\min}$.
- When $\beta = 0$ (enrichment growth exactly offsets net conflict growth), coherence is maintained by *fixed* structure — a knife-edge where the two dynamics balance. Concretely, a system at $\beta = 0$ is an information ecosystem in equilibrium under its current organizing scheme: new data arrives and is absorbed at exactly the rate the existing structure can integrate it, so coherence neither improves nor degrades. This is the state a mature, stable taxonomy achieves — a settled organizational vocabulary handling a steady ingestion rate — and it is precisely as fragile as that description suggests. The balance holds only while $\delta$, $\gamma$, and the ingestion rate all stay put; a shift in the domain, a change in what is being ingested, or the densification discussed below tips $\beta$ off zero, and the equilibrium that felt permanent turns out to have been conditional. A system resting at $\beta = 0$ has no margin, only a coincidence of rates.
- When $\beta < 0$ (enrichment outpaces net conflict growth), coherence *improves* with volume even at fixed structure — more data helps rather than hurts.

This is the substantive correction the two-exponent form buys over the single-exponent version. The earlier treatment carried only $\delta$ and reported that sub-linear enrichment would make the bound "more demanding"; in fact sub-linear enrichment ($\gamma < 1$) *raises* $\beta$, worsening the requirement, while strong clustering ($\delta \to 0$) *lowers* it, and the two can cancel, compound, or invert. A domain with strong clustering but severely diminishing enrichment ($\delta$ small, $\gamma$ small) is not obviously better or worse off than one with weak clustering but near-linear enrichment; the sign of $\beta$ decides, and $\beta$ depends on the gap between the exponents. The severity of the coherence problem is therefore not a single measurable property but a race between two of them. The sign structure is worth stating exactly, because it constrains which regimes are reachable at all: since the raw conflict exponent $1+\delta$ is bounded below by $1$, the composite $\beta = 1+\delta-\gamma$ is negative only when $\gamma > 1+\delta$ and zero only when $\gamma = 1+\delta$ — both requiring *super-linear* enrichment $\gamma > 1$ (with $\gamma = 1$ reaching $\beta = 0$ only in the fully-clustered limit $\delta \to 0$). Merely linear enrichment ($\gamma = 1$) therefore leaves $\beta = \delta \geq 0$: at best it holds coherence steady in the best-clustered case, and otherwise it guarantees decay. The "more data helps for free" regime is thus not a generic possibility but a demanding one, available only where enrichment is genuinely super-linear in volume.

### 3.4.1 A caveat on treating $\delta$ and $\gamma$ as constants

We have written both exponents as fixed, but there is reason to expect at least $\delta$ to drift with scale. Empirically, many real networks *densify* as they grow — the number of edges grows superlinearly in the number of nodes, and average path length shrinks rather than holding constant [Leskovec, Kleinberg & Faloutsos 2007]. Under densification, cross-cluster linkage increases with $V$, which pushes $\delta$ *upward* over time: the small-world property that makes large graphs navigable is the same property that prevents conflicts from staying neatly localized. This means the favorable, strongly-clustered regime ($\delta \to 0$) may be a feature of a corpus's youth rather than a stable property, and a structure that maintains coherence at one scale may fall behind at a larger one purely because $\delta$ has risen under it. We therefore read $\delta$ — and, less certainly, $\gamma$ — as potentially scale-dependent trajectories $\delta(V)$, $\gamma(V)$ rather than constants, and note this directly bears on the community-detection optimism of Section 4.2: community detection lowers the effective $\delta$, but densification works to raise it, and the two are in ongoing tension rather than the former simply winning. Section 8.3's experiment should therefore measure not a single $\delta$ but its trajectory as the corpus grows.

### 3.4.2 The critical volume $V_c$

Making the exponents functions of $V$ turns the composite $\beta(V) = 1 + \delta(V) - \gamma(V)$ into a moving quantity, and this in turn gives the framework a concrete threshold worth naming. Suppose a corpus operates in the benign regime — $\beta(V) < 0$, coherence improving with volume even at fixed structure — at small scale, but $\delta(V)$ rises under densification faster than $\gamma(V)$ rises (or while $\gamma(V)$ decays). Then $\beta(V)$ increases through zero at some **critical volume** $V_c$, defined as the smallest volume at which

$$1 + \delta(V_c) - \gamma(V_c) = 0.$$

For $V < V_c$ the corpus is self-sustaining: its structure, even held fixed, keeps pace with the inconsistency its own growth generates. For $V > V_c$ it is not: $\beta$ has turned positive, the coherence ratio has begun to decay, and maintaining coherence now *requires* structural leverage to grow with volume where before it did not. $V_c$ is thus a **coherence threshold** — a volume past which an architecture that was previously adequate becomes inadequate not because anything was done wrong but because the corpus crossed the scale at which its conflict and enrichment dynamics changed relative order. We deliberately avoid the term "phase transition" here despite the surface resemblance: a phase transition in the physical sense carries specific mathematical signatures — non-analytic behavior of an order parameter, diverging correlation length — that this construction neither requires nor exhibits, and borrowing the term would claim a precision the model does not deliver. What we have is a sign change in an exponent, which is a threshold, not a transition in the physicist's sense. This is a genuinely useful quantity for a practitioner: estimating $\delta(V)$ and $\gamma(V)$ from a corpus's growth history and extrapolating to the root $V_c$ gives an estimate of when the current architecture will break without a change in $S$ — converting the framework's central warning from a qualitative "structure must scale" into a datable prediction of when the failure to scale it will begin to bite. We caution that $V_c$ is only as reliable as the extrapolation of two empirically-fitted, possibly noisy trajectories, and that $\delta(V)$ and $\gamma(V)$ need not be monotone; $V_c$ should be read as an early-warning estimate, not a precise deadline.

### 3.5 Required growth of structure

How fast $S$ itself must grow depends on how leverage responds to structure and on the composite exponent $\beta = 1 + \delta - \gamma$ established above. If $\Lambda(S) \sim S^2$ and $\beta = 1$, then $S \sim \sqrt{V}$ suffices — structure sub-linear in volume. If $\Lambda(S) \sim S$ and $\beta = 1$, $S$ must track $V$ linearly. If $\Lambda(S)$ grows only logarithmically in $S$, the required $S$ grows super-exponentially in $V$ whenever $\beta > 0$, which is effectively unattainable. As $\beta \to 0$, the required growth rate of $S$ relaxes across all three cases, and for $\beta < 0$ no structural growth is required at all. The functional class of $\Lambda$ and the composite exponent $\beta$ are jointly decisive; neither the mere presence of structure nor the mere fact of scale determines the outcome on its own, and — per the densification caveat above — $\beta$ may itself move against the system as the corpus grows.

#### 3.5.1 On the choice of power-law forms

The forms $\epsilon_0 \sim V^{1+\delta}$ and $I \sim V^{\gamma}$ are power laws, and we have justified them only pragmatically, as tractable and intuitive. This is a real assumption, not a neutral one, and it is worth stating plainly what does and does not depend on it. What does *not* depend on the specific power-law form is the paper's central qualitative claim: that the sign of the exponent on $V$ in the coherence ratio — whatever functional class produces it — separates a regime where structure must grow from one where it need not. That claim survives under any pair of monotone growth functions for which a leading-order comparison is meaningful; the power law simply gives the comparison a single clean parameter, $\beta$, to name.

What *does* depend on the form is the severity of the required structural growth, and here one case is genuinely dangerous rather than merely different. If enrichment grows only logarithmically in volume rather than as a power law — $I \sim \log V$ — while inconsistency retains any positive power-law growth, then no attainable growth of structure keeps pace, and the bound becomes vacuously unsatisfiable rather than merely demanding. The paper's more optimistic conclusions quietly assume enrichment is at least weakly polynomial. Whether that assumption holds is an empirical question we do not settle, and it connects directly to a literature we should engage rather than sidestep: the study of scaling laws for machine-learning systems [Kaplan et al. 2020], which characterizes empirically how performance quantities grow with corpus and model size and would bear directly on whether the power-law class, the logarithmic danger case, or something else is typical for the corpora this framework addresses. We flag the sensitivity here and defer the empirical determination to Section 8.3, which is designed to recover both exponents rather than assume them.

## 4. Decoupling Enrichment and Dampening

A simplifying assumption in early formulations of this model sets $\sigma(S) = 1/\phi(S)$, so that a single quantity governs both effects. We now reject that assumption. The two effects are mechanistically distinct and can be present in any combination. This decoupling is what gives the framework descriptive power over real structures.

We organize structures by their effect on the two functions:

**Dampening-dominant** ($\sigma$ decreasing, $\phi \approx 1$). The structure suppresses conflict without generating new signal. A *controlled vocabulary* is the canonical example: fixing the permissible terms eliminates synonymy and ambiguity conflicts, but a vocabulary supports no inference of its own. Uniqueness constraints, validation rules, deduplication, and canonicalization all fall here.

**Enrichment-dominant** ($\phi$ increasing, $\sigma \approx 1$). The structure generates derivable signal without suppressing conflict. *Embeddings* are the clearest case: they induce a similarity geometry that supports inference by proximity, but they impose no consistency — mutually contradictory documents embed side by side without objection. Materialized views over unconstrained base data behave similarly.

**Compound** ($\sigma$ decreasing *and* $\phi$ increasing). The structure does both. *Type systems* and *ontologies with enforced constraints* are the clean cases: a single declared artifact — the type, the class axiom — simultaneously constrains what may be asserted and licenses what may be inferred. *Graphs* are a more qualified member of this quadrant, for a reason worth stating precisely rather than glossing: an edge such as `(Company)-[:ACQUIRED]->(Startup)` is, by itself, purely generative. It asserts a fact and licenses traversal and pattern-matching over it — pure $\phi$. It dampens nothing on its own; nothing prevents a second, contradictory edge from being written unless a genuine a priori constraint — a *declared graph type* that the graph must be an instance of, enforced at write time so that a violating write is rejected — forbids it. This is a stronger and more precise notion than after-the-fact validation — though the axis that matters is *enforcement locus and strength*, not the choice of constraint language. A shape language such as SHACL or ShEx only *specifies* constraints; whether it acts a priori or a posteriori is a deployment decision. Used to report whether an already-existing graph conforms, it is a posteriori inspection; wired as a gate that rejects a nonconforming write, or blocks the change that would introduce one, it enforces a priori for everything that passes through the gate. What distinguishes an ISO-GQL declared graph type is therefore not its language but the strength of its enforcement: mandated by the standard and applied by the engine atomically as part of the write, so the graph cannot enter a forbidden state and no write path routes around the check. A shape gate approximates this discipline but with a weaker guarantee — bypassable by an ungated write, and typically validating a snapshot rather than the transaction itself — which is exactly the effective-versus-nominal dampening gap Section 5.1 develops (we return to it in Section 4.1). The compounding we describe for graphs therefore holds wherever the type discipline is genuinely enforced a priori — most cleanly for an engine-enforced *graph-plus-declared-type* — and not for bare instance data checked only after the fact. We revisit this distinction, and its consequences for the argument in Section 6, directly below.

**Inert** ($\sigma \approx 1$, $\phi \approx 1$). Structure-shaped representations that neither constrain nor generate. *Free-form tagging* is the cautionary case: anyone may attach any tag, so nothing is ruled out, and tags support no systematic inference. Such systems raise $\Lambda$ negligibly and tend to collapse back into the periodic "taxonomy cleanup" cycle.

### 4.1 Consequences of decoupling

The decoupling yields four observations that a single-function model obscures.

First, **dampening has a floor.** $\sigma(S)$ cannot fall below zero conflicts; once a structure has eliminated the conflict classes it can address, further dampening of that kind yields nothing. Dampening-dominant structures therefore have a ceiling on the leverage they can provide, no matter how much of them is added.

Second, **enrichment without dampening amplifies noise.** Because $\phi$ multiplies signal derived from existing content, and because that content may be internally inconsistent, enrichment-dominant structures can propagate and even magnify inconsistency. This is a formal statement of why embedding-only retrieval degrades at scale: it is all $\phi$, no $\sigma$.

Third, **dampening itself splits along an a priori/a posteriori axis, and the two kinds buy leverage in different currencies.** The dampening-dominant examples given above — controlled vocabulary, uniqueness constraints, validation rules — share a property we have so far left implicit: they are all **a priori** or **type-based** structure. The categories are declared before any instance data arrives, and every subsequent fact is checked against a fixed scheme decided in advance. This makes a priori dampening a *filter*: cheap to apply, uniform, and exclusionary — a fact either fits the predeclared type or it is rejected, corrected, or coerced into the nearest available category. The cost of this cheapness is that a priori dampening cannot distinguish "this is inconsistent" from "this is a genuine local exception the scheme did not anticipate." Both are suppressed identically. A controlled vocabulary that has no entry for a legitimate new term treats that term exactly as it would treat noise, because from the filter's point of view there is no difference. This is the dual dissociation to the one in Section 3.1.1, and the other case the intrinsic-coherence stance of Section 1.1 names: here a conformance probe reports incoherence — the term is rejected — where the corpus is intrinsically fine, a false positive to set against the conformant-yet-incoherent false negative, and precisely why conformance is a fallible probe of coherence rather than its arbiter.

**A posteriori** or **shape-based** structure inverts this. Rather than declaring categories in advance, it derives them from the topology of the data itself, after enough of it exists to have a shape — the community detection introduced below is the central example. Because the categories are an output rather than an input, a posteriori structure can preserve local significance that a priori typing would flatten: a small, locally dense cluster can register as a real community regardless of whether it matches any predeclared class, and what would be statistical noise relative to a global a priori schema can still surface as informative if it is locally coherent. The dampening this provides is correspondingly different in kind. It is not exclusionary but *diagnostic*: a new fact that fails to fit any detected community is not rejected, it is flagged as anomalous or as the seed of a new cluster, which is a form of conflict localization rather than conflict suppression. This is a qualitatively different $\sigma$ than a controlled vocabulary's, even though both reduce the effective conflict surface, and the paper's earlier dampening-dominant examples should be read as specifically the a priori case; Section 4.2 develops the a posteriori case directly.

A posteriori structure's diagnostic character is not free, however, and its cost is the mirror image of the a priori filter's cheapness. Because the categories must be derived from data that already exists, a posteriori structure is inherently retrospective: it requires seeing enough of the corpus to detect a shape, which is why it demands the recomputation discussed in Section 5.2. And because the detected shape is a statistical artifact of the current data rather than a declared rule, it does not constrain future writes the way a schema does — it describes what is, at the time of detection, rather than prescribing what may be written next. A priori structure trades flexibility for cheap, immediate, exclusionary dampening at write time; a posteriori structure trades write-time cheapness and prescriptive force for a dampening that is diagnostic, retrospective, and sensitive to local structure a fixed scheme would erase.

Fourth, **sustained coherence favors compound structures or portfolios.** Because each single-mode structure is limited — dampening bottoms out, enrichment amplifies noise, and a priori and a posteriori dampening each have the costs just described — maintaining the required growth of $\Lambda$ over large $V$ generally requires either a compound structure (where the same mechanism does both) or a deliberately composed portfolio of structures (controlled vocabulary *and* schema *and* typed relationships, or a priori schema *and* a posteriori community structure operating together, each covering the other's blind spot).

The dominant contemporary architecture for large-scale retrieval is a concrete instance of exactly this portfolio move, and worth naming because it supplies empirical weight the rest of this section argues for only in principle. Graph-augmented retrieval systems — the GraphRAG family [Edge et al. 2024] — pair an enrichment-dominant substrate (vector embeddings, which raise $\phi$ through geometric induction but supply no dampening) with a posteriori graph structure (community detection over an extracted entity graph, which supplies the localization and hierarchical organization embeddings lack). The reported result is precisely the one the portfolio claim predicts: this composition handles corpus-volume growth on query-focused synthesis tasks where naive vector retrieval alone degrades, because the community layer restores the dampening the embedding layer never had. This is not merely compatible with the framework; it is the framework's central claim — that neither pure enrichment nor pure dampening suffices, and that composing them is what sustains coherence at scale — observed independently in a production-oriented setting. We are careful, however, not to overread it as evidence of restored *dampening* specifically. GraphRAG's reported gains are measured as *comprehensiveness* and *diversity* — enrichment-side ($\phi$) quantities, adjudicated by LLM pairwise comparison on two relatively narrow corpora and with the evaluation-bias caveats its authors themselves note — not as the self-consistency measures that would show conflict being suppressed. It is therefore an existence proof that the portfolio *composition* is adopted and useful, not a controlled demonstration that its benefit runs through dampening rather than through better retrieval alone. Section 8.6 sets out exactly the self-consistency-versus-task-success decomposition that would license the stronger reading, and the framework should hold its own headline example to that bar rather than count enrichment-side wins as dampening evidence. It is worth noting, per Section 4.2.1, that GraphRAG reaches its a posteriori dampening through the *graph's* topology, but that clustering is the general mechanism and could in principle be reached geometrically, over the embedding itself; the graph is one particularly clean route to the localization, not the only one.

This is a more qualified case for graph-structured information than we initially posed it. It is not that a bare graph is the *most* structure, nor that dampening and enrichment arise from one indivisible construct in every graph — Section 4's discussion of the compound quadrant already conceded that the edge itself is generative and that dampening depends on an additional, separately-enforced constraint. The defensible version of the claim is narrower, and the a priori/a posteriori distinction just drawn is exactly what sharpens it: a graph governed by a *declared graph type* — as standardized in ISO-GQL and implemented in Cypher, where the graph is an instance of a type enforced at write time and a violating write is rejected — brings dampening and enrichment into close proximity, declared over the same typed vocabulary of node and relationship labels, in a way that an unconstrained property graph does not achieve on its own. This is genuinely a priori structure in the sense of Section 4.1: the type is declared in advance and every subsequent write is checked against it and rejected if it does not conform, exactly as a static type rejects an ill-typed program. It should not be conflated with shape *validation* (SHACL, ShEx) used as after-the-fact inspection, which reports nonconformance once a graph already exists — a legitimate but a posteriori, detect-and-flag dampening of the kind discussed in Section 4.2. As Section 4 notes, the same shape language wired as a write-gate instead enforces a priori, though with a weaker, bypassable guarantee than an engine-enforced type; what earns the graph its place in the compound quadrant is a priori enforcement of the declared type, however implemented, with the ISO-GQL engine-enforced form as its strongest instance, and not instance data validated only retrospectively. An unconstrained property graph is enrichment-dominant, not compound; it earns the compound classification only once governed by a declared type, at which point the write-time cost discussed in Section 5.1 applies in full.

### 4.2 Community structure as unqualified, a posteriori compound structure

The graph case above required a schema qualification because a bare edge is purely generative. Topological community structure — the partition of a graph into densely-connected clusters via modularity optimization, label propagation, or related methods — does not require this qualification, and is worth treating as a distinct entry in the taxonomy rather than folded into the general graph discussion. It is also, per Section 4.1's third observation, the paper's central example of the **a posteriori** side of the type-based/shape-based distinction, in contrast to the a priori dampening-dominant examples (controlled vocabulary, uniqueness constraints) given earlier.

Community detection takes relationships already present in the graph and derives a new structural object from their aggregate shape: cluster membership. No single edge states which community a node belongs to; the assignment is a regularity of the topology of many relationships considered together, discovered after the fact rather than declared in advance. Per Section 3.3.1, this is enrichment produced by a fixed, rule-governed procedure with no independent model interposing a fallible synthesis step — the property that keeps it inside the compound quadrant — but it is not inference in the narrow, local sense that term is reserved for. Community detection is *partitioning*: it collapses many nodes into one group, and the community membership is a reduce-shaped summary of the neighborhood, not a pointwise derivation from a pair of statements. Section 4.3 develops a second, non-reducing shape-based mechanism — motif detection — that belongs to the same rule-governed family without sharing this partitioning character.

The dampening effect is what makes this a genuinely unqualified compound case, and it is diagnostic rather than exclusionary in exactly the sense Section 4.1 distinguishes. Community assignment gives every new fact somewhere to be checked, but it does not reject what fails to fit — it flags it. A new relationship either reinforces an existing community — increasing its internal density, consistent with the pattern already detected — or it fails to fit any existing community well, which is itself informative: either a novel cluster is forming, or the new fact is anomalous relative to the graph's prevailing topology and merits scrutiny. Unlike a controlled vocabulary's a priori filter, which would suppress an unrecognized term identically whether it is noise or a legitimate exception, community structure preserves the misfit as a visible signal rather than erasing it. This is conflict *localization* in concrete, mechanical form. Rather than checking a new fact against all $V$ existing facts, the natural comparison set is the fact's community, whose size is typically $O(1)$ relative to $V$ once the graph is large. Community structure does not merely benefit from the clustering correction introduced in Section 3.2 — it **actively produces** the favorable $\delta$ that correction assumed. Section 3.2 treated $\delta$ as a property of the domain to be measured; community detection is a mechanism for *engineering* $\delta$ downward, converting a hoped-for property of the data into a maintained property of the structure.

Both effects — the derived membership fact and the localized comparison set it enables — arise from the same detected partition. There is no separate schema doing the dampening work while the instance data does the enrichment work, as there was for the bare-edge case in Section 4.1. This makes community structure, not the type system or the schema-qualified graph, arguably the cleanest illustration of the compound quadrant in this taxonomy: a single derived artifact that simultaneously narrows the effective conflict surface and exposes new derivable signal, with no auxiliary constraint layer required to make the dampening real. Communities of communities — re-partitioning the graph whose nodes are already-detected communities — are the same partitioning mechanism applied recursively at coarser granularity, and inherit the same reduce-shaped character; we distinguish this from the motif-of-motifs hierarchy developed next, which is structure-preserving rather than partitioning at every level.

#### 4.2.1 The same localization by geometry and by indexing dimension

Community detection localizes conflict by graph topology, but topology is not the only coordinate along which a global conflict can be made local, and it is worth stating the general move because two other cases matter for the systems this framework addresses. The general move is: *find a coordinate along which statements that globally appear to conflict are separated into different neighborhoods, and check consistency within a neighborhood rather than across the whole corpus.* Community detection uses topology; two further cases use geometry and indexing.

The **geometric** case is similarity clustering over an embedding. Section 4's taxonomy files a bare embedding as enrichment-dominant — proximity raises $\phi$ by geometric induction, and nothing rejects a contradictory neighbor — and that remains correct of the bare embedding. But clustering *over* the embedding is a posteriori structure of exactly the community-detection kind, reached through geometric proximity rather than edge topology: a cluster is a derived categorical fact present in no single vector, and it supplies the same $O(1)$ local comparison set that engineers $\delta$ downward. So an embedding indexed and then clustered is not enrichment-only; the clustering layer is a posteriori compound structure, and the "enrichment-dominant" classification belongs to the raw embedding before that layer is added. When a new statement lands in a cluster, similarity establishes only that the statement is *about* the same thing as its neighbors — and that aboutness is precisely the precondition for three distinct outcomes the earlier fit/misfit binary collapses: the statement may **validate** a neighbor (same claim, same polarity — redundancy that raises confidence, dampening as positive detection), **extend** the neighborhood (a non-contradictory addition — enrichment), or **contradict** a neighbor (same neighborhood, opposite polarity — soft inconsistency surfaced). Which of the three it is cannot be read from proximity, because proximity is evidence of relevance, not of agreement: two statements can be near-neighbors precisely because they make opposite claims about the same proposition. This is why the geometric mechanism's dampening is strictly diagnostic — it raises the recall of candidate contradictions for inspection but cannot adjudicate them — and why it stays firmly on the a posteriori, flag-don't-reject side.

The **indexing** case is subtler and shows that time and provenance are dampening structure, not merely metadata. Many apparent contradictions are artifacts of having projected away a dimension along which the statements were in fact consistent. "Likes pepperoni" and "does not like pepperoni" is a global contradiction only if time is projected out; index the two statements by *when* they held and they occupy different temporal neighborhoods, both true in their slice, the later revising rather than contradicting the earlier. "I did not eat it" and "a witness saw me eat it" is a global contradiction only if provenance is projected out; index by *source* and they are two differently-authoritative claims about one proposition, to be weighed rather than resolved by logic. Restoring such a dimension is the same localization move as clustering — it separates a conflicting pair into different neighborhoods so that consistency can be checked locally — with the separating coordinate being temporal or provenantial rather than topological or geometric. Time and provenance are, in this framing, additional axes along which the effective conflict surface is made local and the effective $\delta$ is lowered; a corpus that stores them is structurally better dampened than one that has flattened them away, and a large share of what presents as soft inconsistency in practice is unindexed revision or unattributed disagreement that the fuller, higher-dimensional representation would never have registered as conflict. This is a structural fix in the paper's exact sense: dampening by adding the dimension that makes a global conflict locally consistent.

For a continuously-updating agentic memory, the adjudication these mechanisms set up — ordering revisions by time, weighting disagreements by provenance, promoting validated clusters, isolating the contradictions that survive both — is naturally performed offline rather than at write time, and is the agentic-memory instance of the recomputation that Section 5.2 argues is a feature rather than a defect for such systems.

### 4.3 Motifs and compositional hierarchy: shape-based structure without reduction

Community detection is not the only shape-based, a posteriori mechanism, and its reduce-shaped character — collapsing nodes into groups — is not representative of the whole family. A second mechanism, **motif detection**, recognizes recurring, nameable configurations of relationships — a triangle, a feed-forward loop, a star, a bipartite core — without collapsing or summarizing the underlying relationships away. A motif is a new structural entity, visible only at the level of the pattern rather than at the level of any single edge, but it coexists with the fine-grained relationships that compose it rather than replacing them. Where community detection asks "which group does this node belong to," motif detection asks "which small, recurring shape does this local neighborhood instantiate," and answering the second question leaves every underlying node and edge exactly where it was.

This distinction is not academic; it changes what kind of dampening the mechanism provides. Community detection's dampening operates at the grain of group membership: a new fact is checked against the community it would join. Motif detection's dampening operates at a finer, more domain-legible grain: it treats a recurring motif as an implicit expectation about which configurations of relationships normally co-occur. If a domain is dominated by a particular motif — mutual-friend triangles in a social network, feed-forward loops in a regulatory network, reciprocal citation structures in a scholarly one — then an edge that would complete a known motif is unsurprising and reinforcing, while an edge that produces an unusual, non-motif-conforming local configuration is precisely the signal worth flagging. This is diagnostic dampening at a finer resolution than community-level anomaly detection, and it more closely resembles the way domain experts actually reason about structural surprise: not "this doesn't belong to any known group" but "that is not how families work" or "that is an unusual supply-chain configuration" — judgments made against a specific, small, named pattern rather than a large partition.

The recursive step is where this mechanism connects most directly to the motivating question behind this section. Once motifs are detected and labeled, they define a new graph — call it the motif graph — whose nodes are motif instances and whose edges represent adjacency, shared participation, or containment between them. This motif graph can itself contain recurring patterns, detectable by the same procedure applied one level up: motifs of motifs. Composition proceeds indefinitely in principle, each level built from patterns detected in the level below, with no partitioning or information loss at any step — every original relationship remains intact underneath however many layers of motif structure have been recognized on top of it.

What makes this more than a formal curiosity is that many domains have already converged, independently and long before graph theory formalized any of this, on names for the specific motifs and motif-compositions that recur in their subject matter. A nuclear family is a small, specific, densely-connected motif among individual relationships. An extended kinship network is a motif of family-motifs. A friend group, a firm, a market, an institution, a society — each is plausibly best understood not as an arbitrary label imposed on a detected cluster, but as the name a domain has given to a recurring configuration at a specific level of exactly this compositional hierarchy, arrived at through long human experience with the pattern rather than through an algorithm. This gives the shape-based side of the taxonomy an unusually strong form of external validation: the levels of structure this framework predicts should be discoverable are, in many domains, already named, which is evidence that motif-based composition is describing something domains have independently found useful to track, rather than imposing a structure they do not otherwise recognize.

The coherence bound of Section 3.4 applies at every level of this hierarchy, recursively, exactly as it does for communities of communities in Section 4.2 — but because motif detection does not partition or discard the finer-grained levels beneath it, a system built this way retains the ability to reason at whichever level of the hierarchy is relevant to a given query, rather than being committed to a single, fixed resolution the way a one-shot community partition is. This is a genuine advantage of the motif-based approach for systems that need to move fluidly between fine-grained and coarse-grained reasoning — the individual relationship and the named institution — without having irreversibly discarded one level to obtain the other.

Both mechanisms connect directly to Section 6's placement of graphs at the top of the prose-to-graph continuum. Communities and motifs alike are best understood as further rungs *above* the graph itself — structure imposed on top of a graph specifically to manage the volume problem at the graph's own scale, recursively, whether by partitioning (Section 4.2) or by pattern-preserving composition (this section). Nothing in the taxonomy stops at the graph; a graph with either mechanism layered on top is a higher rung still, and the continuum of Section 6 should be read as extending past its eighth, graph-labeled entry rather than terminating there.

## 5. What "Adding Structure" Means

The decoupling of Section 4 lets us be precise about a phrase that is otherwise vague. "Adding structure" is not a single act with a single effect. It is the selection of a point in the $(\phi, \sigma)$ space, with consequences that depend entirely on which mechanism is chosen.

We can now restate the interventions of Section 2 in these terms. Re-ranking and prefiltering in retrieval are principally dampening operations layered atop an enrichment-dominant substrate (embeddings); they supply the $\sigma$ that vectors lack. PageRank is an enrichment operation (it derives an authority signal not present in any single page) that also dampens (it demotes the spam that saturated the relevance signal). Type systems are compound by construction.

This reframing also connects to the classical engineering tradeoff between computation, storage, and freshness. Each structural mechanism is, in effect, a decision about *when* reasoning work is performed. Prefiltering moves conflict resolution from query time to index time. Materialized inference (embeddings, derived views, stored graph relationships) moves signal generation from read time to write time. Structure, in this light, is *materialized reasoning*: work performed once, upstream, and amortized over many downstream uses — where the amortization is not merely reuse but active read-time optimization, the subject of Section 5.3.

The cost of materialization is freshness. Pre-computed structure reflects the corpus as it was at materialization time and decays as the corpus drifts. The governing tradeoff is therefore not merely compute versus storage but **leverage versus freshness**: richer pre-computed structure yields higher $\Lambda$ but decays faster and costs more to refresh. This yields a practical decision rule — materialize structure when the domain is stable enough that the structure will not go stale before it repays its cost — and explains, for instance, why retrieval-augmented approaches persist alongside fine-tuning: they trade leverage for currency.

### 5.1 A second cost the materialization framing omits: write-time coordination

The account above treats $\sigma(S)$ as though it were free once materialized — a passive shield inspected at read time and otherwise costless. This is not correct, and the omission matters enough to name directly. Enforcing dampening is not merely a matter of encoding a constraint; it is a matter of *checking* that constraint against a growing, and possibly distributed, body of existing statements every time a new one is written. A global uniqueness constraint, a class-disjointness rule, or a check that a proposed relationship does not create a contradictory cycle all require the system to consult — in the worst case — the entire relevant portion of the existing corpus at write time. This cost rises with $V$ independently of the coherence bound derived above, and in distributed systems it further competes with write throughput and horizontal scalability, since strong dampening often requires coordination (locking, consensus, or serialized validation) across whatever partitions the data spans.

This gives materialization a second axis of cost, alongside freshness, that the leverage-versus-freshness framing in isolation does not capture: **leverage versus write latency**. A structure that offers high $\Lambda$ through aggressive dampening may be economically or operationally unusable if the coordination cost of enforcing it at write time exceeds what the system's throughput requirements allow. This is not a minor caveat. It means the choice of how much $\sigma$ to build into a system is bounded not only by whether the structure will go stale (Section 5's freshness point) but by whether the corpus can absorb writes fast enough to remain useful while the structure is enforced. In practice, systems often resolve this by relaxing dampening at write time — eventual consistency, deferred validation, best-effort constraints — which quietly reduces the effective $\sigma(S)$ below what the schema nominally promises. Any application of the bound in Section 3.4 to a real system should use the *effective*, enforced $\sigma$, not the *nominal*, declared one; the two can diverge substantially under write-heavy or highly distributed workloads.

For readers coming from distributed systems, this maps onto familiar territory. Enforcing hard a priori dampening at write time is a global-state verification: a uniqueness constraint or a cross-entity consistency check must, in the limit, observe the whole relevant partition before admitting a write, which is the requirement for strict serializability and the strong-consistency corner of the CAP tradeoff. Maximizing $\sigma$ at write time therefore tends toward ACID serializability and its throughput ceiling; relaxing $\sigma$ toward eventual consistency or deferring it to a posteriori detection (Section 5.2) is a move toward a BASE model, trading enforced-at-write dampening for availability and write throughput. Read in this light, the leverage-versus-write-latency axis is not a new tradeoff but the coherence-framework's name for the consistency-versus-availability dial database engineers already tune: strong-consistency isolation buys high effective $\sigma$ at the cost of write throughput, weak isolation buys throughput at the cost of effective $\sigma$, and a posteriori dampening is the option that recovers some of the lost dampening *after* the write, off the critical path, at the cost of a window during which the structure is not yet current.

### 5.2 Recomputation cost as a feature of the target system, not a defect of the technique

Section 4.2's community structure inherits this cost directly, and it is worth resisting the instinct to treat it as a shortcoming. Community assignment is not computed once and fixed; as new relationships are written, a node's community membership can change, communities can split or merge, and detection may need to be re-run, incrementally or in full, to keep the structure faithful to the data it describes. Under the framing of Section 5.1, this looks like a straightforward cost: the leverage community structure provides is bought with a recurring recomputation bill that scales with write volume.

We think this framing undersells what is actually happening, at least for one important and growing class of system. The recomputation requirement is a defect only if the target is a **static system of record** — a corpus whose useful lifetime is spent mostly at rest, where structure is built once against a largely fixed body of data and queried many times before it is revisited. Against that target, yes, community structure's need for maintenance is overhead. But an increasing share of the systems this framework is meant to describe are not that target at all. Agentic systems, and in particular the memory layers such systems rely on, are continuously ingesting new observations, revising prior beliefs, and are expected to reflect that revision in their retrievable structure promptly — not eventually, and not only at the next scheduled rebuild. For these systems, the demand is not for a structure that is cheap to leave alone; it is for a structure that is cheap, or at least tractable, to keep current.

Under that lens, the recomputation cost of community detection is not a tax on an otherwise-static asset — it is the price of admission for the kind of structure a dynamic, adaptive system actually needs, and the relevant comparison is not "static schema, no recomputation cost" versus "communities, recomputation cost," but "structure that can be kept current under continuous write pressure" versus "structure that cannot be, and therefore silently goes stale in exactly the systems that can least afford it." A type system checked once at compile time is cheap precisely because the artifact it governs — a compiled program — is not expected to keep changing after deployment. An agentic memory is closer to the opposite case: the corpus *is* the deployment, changing continuously, and structure that assumes a quiescent base is a poor fit regardless of how cheap it is to compute once. Incremental community-detection algorithms, and the broader move toward maintaining structure online rather than recomputing it in batch, are best read as the natural response to this shift in what kind of system is being served — not as an optimization of the technique described in Section 4.2, but as a sign that the technique is being asked to operate in the regime it is actually suited for.

This reframes the leverage-versus-write-latency tradeoff of Section 5.1 for this class of system: the cost is not weighed against a competing preference for cheap writes to a stable store, but against the alternative of a system of record that cannot adapt fast enough to remain a faithful description of what an agent currently believes. For a dynamic memory system, the coherence bound of Section 3.4 is not a one-time design constraint checked at build time; it is a standing operational requirement that the leverage $\Lambda(S(V))$ must continue to satisfy as $V$ grows *during operation*, which in turn requires that $S$ itself be maintained, not merely built.

### 5.3 Structure as an optimization surface: the other side of the ledger

Sections 5.1 and 5.2 develop only the cost side of structure's compute budget — the write-time coordination and recomputation that enforcing and maintaining structure demand. Read alone, they leave the misleading impression that structure is a net compute *sink*, paid for entirely in freshness and write latency. It is not, and the completing observation is that the very same knowledge of logical structure that costs compute to enforce is what *earns* compute back at read time. Structure is not only a cost; it is an optimization surface, and the more a system knows and can guarantee about its own logical structure, the more aggressively it can optimize against it.

This operates through two distinct channels. The first is **query planning.** An optimizer's leverage is a direct function of what the structure lets it assume: declared types allow it to skip runtime checks; uniqueness and cardinality constraints let it choose join strategies and short-circuit once a match is found; a known schema lets it prune search paths and select indexes; functional dependencies let it eliminate provably redundant work. An unstructured store can guarantee nothing about the shape of its data and must therefore treat every query defensively, at worst-case cost. Each guarantee the structure supplies shrinks the query-time search space the planner must consider. The second is **supplemental acceleration mechanisms.** Known structure is the precondition for building auxiliary accelerators at all — one can only index a field known to exist, materialize a view whose consistency can be maintained, partition along a boundary the structure guarantees, or cache with correct invalidation derived from known dependencies. These mechanisms do not merely benefit from structure; they are impossible without it.

The important point for this paper is that this is not a separate consideration bolted onto Section 5's materialization framing — it is the read-time half of the same coin whose write-time half Section 5.1 described. Structure moves work from read time to write time *precisely by* giving the optimizer enough guaranteed logical structure to pre-plan, pre-index, and pre-compute. The write-time coordination cost of 5.1 and the read-time optimization benefit of this subsection are the same act of materialization seen from opposite ends: what is paid at write time as enforcement is recovered at read time as the guarantees that make optimization sound. This completes the "where does the work live" thesis of Section 5 in its strongest form: structure does not merely relocate work upstream, it lets a system *choose* where work lives, and read-time optimization is the return on having chosen to pay upstream.

This benefit is not free-standing, however, and its availability is bounded by exactly the effective-versus-nominal distinction drawn in Section 5.1. An optimizer can only exploit a guarantee it can *rely on*: a constraint that is declared but not actually enforced — relaxed under write pressure, deferred, or maintained only on a best-effort basis — gives the planner nothing it can safely plan against, because a guarantee that might be violated is not a guarantee at all. The read-time optimization payoff is therefore available only to the extent that the write-time enforcement cost was actually paid. This ties the optimization benefit directly back to the effective $\sigma$ of Section 5.1 rather than the nominal one: a system that relaxes enforcement to protect write throughput does not merely risk admitting inconsistency, it also forfeits the read-time optimizations that the foregone guarantees would have licensed. The two sides of the ledger are enforced by the same mechanism, and relaxing it surrenders both at once.

## 6. A Continuum From Prose to Graphs

We now place a range of representations on a single axis, ordered roughly by increasing structural commitment, and track how $\phi$ and $\sigma$ evolve along it. We run a parallel to programming-language type disciplines, because that parallel is the version of this argument most engineers have already lived.

1. **Prose.** $\sigma \approx 1$, $\phi \approx 1$. Nothing constrains what may be said; contradiction is grammatically permissible. All dampening and all inference reside in the human reader. This is precisely the regime in which the interpretive dampening of Section 3 operates — and, per the bound, precisely the regime that fails first at scale.

2. **Structured prose** (sections, headings, citations). Mild $\sigma$ reduction: conflicting claims can at least be localized and cross-referenced. Negligible $\phi$. Parallel: code formatting conventions — no change to semantics, an aid to human dampening.

3. **Controlled vocabulary.** $\sigma$ reduced, $\phi \approx 1$. The dampening-dominant archetype of Section 4. Parallel: reserved keywords and naming conventions, which remove classes of misreading without deriving anything.

4. **Markup / key–value** (JSON, YAML). Mild $\sigma$ reduction and the first weak $\phi$: keyed access makes mechanical query possible, but values remain unconstrained. Parallel: dynamically typed languages — but the parallel must be stated carefully, because dynamic typing is not simply "less structure." It is a *trade* along the two axes rather than a reduction on one: dynamic languages relax write-time $\sigma$ (constraints are checked late or never, so a type error surfaces only at the moment of failure) but frequently purchase *additional* $\phi$ with the freedom that relaxation buys — metaprogramming, duck typing, runtime introspection, and monkey-patching all generate behavior and derivable structure that a rigid static discipline forecloses. This is the decoupling of Section 4 seen inside a programming language: dynamic typing moves *down* on $\sigma$ and often *up* on $\phi$ simultaneously, which is precisely why it is not strictly dominated by static typing and why the choice between them is a real engineering tension rather than a matter of one being more "structured" than the other.

   *Off-axis, near this rung: vector embeddings.* Embeddings do not sit cleanly on this commitment-ordered axis, because they are high-$\phi$, low-$\sigma$ in a way the axis's monotone increase in both factors does not accommodate — but if placed, they belong around here, alongside markup, as a *geometric key–value store* whose keys are latent vectors rather than literal strings. The *bare* embedding offers more $\phi$ than markup (proximity supports similarity-based recall, a genuine if non-inferential enrichment per Section 3.3.1) but essentially no more $\sigma$ (nothing rejects or flags a contradictory neighbor), which is why the raw embedding is the sharpest contrast to the relational and graph rungs below: maximal geometric enrichment, negligible dampening, and exactly why the portfolio move of Section 4.1 pairs it *with* dampening-bearing structure rather than using it alone. This characterization is of the bare embedding only: as Section 4.2.1 argues, similarity clustering layered *over* the embedding is a posteriori compound structure of the same kind as community detection, so an embedding that has been clustered is no longer enrichment-only — it has acquired diagnostic dampening geometrically.

5. **Schema** (JSON Schema, XML DTD). Stronger $\sigma$: malformed data is rejected at the boundary. $\phi$ still thin — schemas mostly validate. Parallel: runtime type-checking and gradual typing in loose configurations.

6. **Type systems.** $\sigma$ strong *and* $\phi$ rising — the crossover at which structure turns from defensive to generative. A sufficiently expressive type system not only rejects ill-formed states but derives: inferred types, resolved instances, parametricity guarantees. There is a gradient *within* languages here, from types that principally constrain memory layout to types that substantially generate code and guarantees.

7. **Relational model.** Strong $\sigma$ via normalization — each fact stored once, so it cannot disagree with itself; conflict is prevented by construction rather than detected after the fact. This by-construction guarantee is narrower than it first appears, and the distinction matters for the continuum's ordering: normalization prevents *structural* redundancy and the update anomalies that follow from it (a fact stored in one place cannot fall out of sync with a duplicate copy, because there is no duplicate copy), but it does not prevent a user from entering two contradictory *semantic* facts in two separate, individually well-formed rows [Abadi et al. 2007 for the scaling context; the anomaly taxonomy is classical normalization theory]. The relational model dampens the hard, structural inconsistency of duplication; it does not on its own dampen the soft, semantic inconsistency of two rows that are each valid but jointly implausible — which is precisely the gap a graph-plus-enforced-schema, and above it the a posteriori mechanisms of Sections 4.2–4.3, are positioned to close, and part of why they sit higher on the continuum. $\phi$ comes from the relational algebra, but with a characteristic cost: the join derives related facts *at query time, on every query*. Relationships are recomputed rather than stored.

8. **Graphs.** $\phi$ is materialized upstream: relationships are stored and traversed rather than recomputed at query time, as the relational model requires. But — per the correction in Section 4 — this enrichment is present in the bare instance data regardless of whether dampening is. Strong $\sigma$ is retained from schema and identity only insofar as a declared graph type (node and relationship element types, with property existence, type, uniqueness, and key constraints) is enforced at write time; an unconstrained property graph inherits none of the relational model's by-construction dampening and must earn it separately. This is where the programming-language parallel stops being an analogy and becomes literal: a graph type in the ISO-GQL sense — as implemented in Cypher, via `ALTER CURRENT GRAPH TYPE SET`, where a write that violates the type is rejected and the graph is required to *be an instance of* the type — is to a property graph precisely what a static type is to a value. The graph cannot enter a state the type forbids, rather than being inspected afterward for whether it did; a shape language such as SHACL provides that inspection when used to report conformance after the fact, and belongs on the a posteriori side then — though, as Section 4 notes, the same language wired as a write-gate enforces a priori, with a weaker, bypassable guarantee than the engine-enforced graph type. What places this rung here is a priori enforcement of the type, strongest in the ISO-GQL form, not the particular language the constraint is written in. Where the declared type is present, the two effects compound, because both are expressed over the same typed vocabulary of node and relationship labels. Where it is absent, a graph is enrichment-dominant in exactly the sense embeddings are, and the compounding claimed for the endpoint of this continuum does not yet hold. In the materialization framing of Section 5, the graph stores the relationship where the relational model recomputes it — the compute-versus-storage decision applied to relationships themselves — but that framing describes $\phi$ alone, not the compound case. This entry is not the continuum's terminus: Sections 4.2 and 4.3 describe two further, shape-based rungs built on top of a graph — community partitioning and motif composition, respectively — each of which layers additional, a posteriori structure over whatever a priori type the graph already carries.

Two observations follow.

**The continuum is not monotone in a single variable.** Dampening rises fairly steadily along the axis, but enrichment is jagged: it spikes at type systems, dips at the pure relational model (query-time joins), and spikes again at graphs. The lesson is that reaching coherence is not a matter of "more structure" but of structure of the right *kind* — a point the single-function model could not express.

**The programming-language parallel is itself evidence.** The industry's drift toward static and gradual typing as systems and teams grow is the bound observed in the field: teams adopt types not for their own sake but when volume — of code, of contributors — makes incoherence intolerable. The dynamic-to-static gradient is the leverage-versus-freshness tradeoff experienced from the inside: dynamic code defers dampening (cheap now, incoherent later); static code pays upstream (lead time now, leverage thereafter). But the endpoints are the least interesting part of the parallel, and it would be a mistake to read the continuum as a simple choice between them. The genuinely instructive case is **gradual typing** — the machinery, studied formally since Siek and Taha and argued for programmatically by Meijer and Drayton under the banner of "static typing where possible, dynamic typing when needed," that lets a single system apply strong $\sigma$ to the regions where incoherence is intolerable while leaving other regions dynamic to retain the $\phi$ that flexibility buys. Gradual typing is not a midpoint on a one-dimensional scale; it is the direct linguistic analogue of this paper's central recommendation, that sustained coherence is reached by a deliberately composed *portfolio* rather than by maximizing a single structural mode (Section 4.1). A gradually-typed codebase is a portfolio in exactly the paper's sense: a priori type constraints where the conflict surface is dense and costly, dynamic flexibility where enrichment matters more than dampening, and an explicit boundary — the gradual type system's runtime checks at the typed/untyped frontier — managing the seam between them. That per-region allocation of $\sigma$, rather than the global static/dynamic choice, is what the continuum is really about, and it is why the type-system parallel is not merely illustrative but structurally the same problem the rest of the paper describes.

## 7. Relation to Information Theory

The framework's central terms — *information*, *signal*, *noise* — are borrowed from a vocabulary that information theory has made precise. It is therefore reasonable to ask whether this is an application of information theory, a compatible extension of it, or merely a reuse of its words. The short answer is that the framework is *not* a corollary of Shannon's theory — our $I(V)$ is semantic, not Shannon information, and the volume dynamics have no counterpart in a static channel model — but that it is not therefore disconnected: two static bridges (to error-correcting redundancy and to mutual information) are defensible, and a third, to Minimum Description Length, is the one that plausibly reaches the volume dynamics and is the most promising direction for future formalization. A reader interested only in the framework's positive content can take that summary and skip to Section 8; the subsections below are for readers who want the connections drawn carefully, and are ordered so that the MDL bridge (7.4), which we regard as the most valuable, builds on the terminological groundwork before it.

### 7.1 A necessary terminological distinction

Shannon information measures the reduction of uncertainty from observing a symbol drawn from a distribution; it is deliberately independent of meaning. The quantity we have written $I(V)$ is the opposite in this one respect: it is *semantic* signal — coherent, non-contradictory, load-bearing content — and its whole point is meaning. There is prior work that takes semantic content as its explicit object rather than setting it aside — Floridi's theory of strongly semantic information, for instance, defines information in terms of well-formed, meaningful, and *truthful* data, and treats contradiction as carrying zero or negative informational value [Floridi 2004] — and our $I(V)$ is closer in spirit to that lineage than to Shannon's. We invoke it for that kinship of spirit only, and are explicit about the limit of the claim: we do not adopt Floridi's formal apparatus, and this paper does not rest on it. We note, rather than use, that this apparatus is a candidate source of a more principled form for $\epsilon$ — the theory quantifies semantic discrepancy in a way our count of contradictions only approximates — and mark engaging it properly as future work rather than implying we have done so here. The signal/noise framing we adopt is older and broader than Shannon's channel model and does not, on its own, invoke channel capacity, entropy of a source, or any rate. We therefore do not claim that $I(V)$ is a Shannon quantity, and the results of Section 3 are not corollaries of Shannon's theorems. Stating this plainly is a precondition for making the two narrower claims that follow, which are genuine.

### 7.2 Dampening as error-correcting redundancy

The stronger bridge concerns the dampening factor $\sigma(S)$. Shannon's channel-coding theorem establishes that reliable communication over a noisy channel is achieved by adding *structured redundancy* — codes whose constraints allow errors to be detected and corrected. Structural dampening is mechanistically analogous in this narrow sense: a schema asserts the same rule across many records; a uniqueness constraint functions as a parity check; a type excludes invalid symbols from a stream. In each case, structure introduces redundancy that renders certain inconsistencies detectable and rejectable, exactly as an error-correcting code renders certain corruptions recoverable.

We are careful to bound the claim at the *structural* level, because the *causal* level does not transfer. Classical channel coding addresses random corruption of an otherwise-fixed, well-defined message — bit flips introduced by a noisy medium. The inconsistency that accumulates in a growing corpus is rarely of this kind. It arises instead from compounding *semantic* divergence: business logic that changes underfoot, human definitions that conflict because they were authored under different assumptions, terms whose meaning drifts over time. A schema constraint detects the resulting violation exactly as a parity check detects a flipped bit, but it does not diagnose or repair *why* the violation arose, in the way that decoding a corrected message recovers the sender's original intent. The bridge therefore holds as a claim about detection-via-redundancy — dampening treats the symptom, structurally, in the same way a code does — and should not be read as a claim that structure corrects semantic drift the way coding corrects channel noise. It does not; it only makes the drift's consequences visible and rejectable.

There is a second, subtler place where the analogy must be handled with care, concerning the *direction* in which redundancy acts. In a communication channel, error-correcting redundancy is added by *increasing* the volume of transmitted symbols — extra parity bits accompany the message so the original can be recovered despite noise; coding expands the signal to protect it. A priori structural dampening appears to run the opposite way: a constraint *reduces* the set of acceptable inputs, rejecting data at ingestion rather than adding anything to protect it. On its face the mechanics are inverted — coding maximizes throughput over a fixed-meaning channel by adding bits, while a priori dampening minimizes noise by refusing bits. The resolution is that the redundancy in the structural case lives not in the data stream but in the *schema*: a single declared constraint is asserted, redundantly, against every record that must satisfy it, and it is that one-against-many redundancy — the constraint repeated implicitly across all instances it governs — that plays the role the parity bits play in the channel. So the analogy survives, but only once relocated: the redundancy is in the constraint's uniform applicability, not in an expansion of the corpus, and the visible *rejection* of nonconforming inputs is the detection event, not the redundancy itself. This relocation matters because it holds only for a priori dampening; a posteriori dampening (Sections 4.2–4.3) does not reject inputs at all, so the coding analogy applies to it more weakly still — it detects and flags without the exclusion that made the inversion apparent in the first place.

This is nonetheless more than an analogy of convenience, because the narrower claim is still productive. It converts the otherwise-assumed form of $\sigma(S)$ into a question with a coding-theoretic shape: *is there a bound on how much inconsistency a given quantity of structural redundancy can suppress, regardless of the inconsistency's semantic origin?* An affirmative answer would supply $\sigma(S)$ with a principled functional form in place of the illustrative one used here, and would tighten the leverage bound of Section 3 accordingly. We do not derive such a bound in this draft, but we note that the dampening half of our framework is the half most likely to admit a rigorous information-theoretic treatment, and we flag it as the most promising direction for formal development — with both the causal caveat and the relocation of redundancy above kept firmly attached to any future claim built on it.

### 7.3 Enrichment as accessible mutual information

The weaker bridge concerns specifically the **inferential** subset of enrichment distinguished in Section 3.3.1, not $\phi(S)$ as a whole, and we advance it as a reading rather than a result. When structure derives a fact by rule — inferring $A \to C$ from $A \to B$ and $B \to C$ — it exploits the fact that the stored statements are not independent. In information-theoretic terms, there is mutual information among them. Inferential enrichment does not create information; it makes mutual information that is already latent in the corpus *accessible* as a derived fact. Unstructured storage leaves that mutual information inert; rule-governed structure is what renders it usable. This reading is appealing and may be formalizable, but we do not claim a quantitative correspondence between $\phi(S)$ and any specific mutual-information measure, and we caution against over-reading it. It also does not extend to the non-inferential portion of $\phi(S)$: enrichment by geometric induction (embeddings) or by generative synthesis (model-produced summaries) raises usable signal through mechanisms this bridge does not cover, and would require separate information-theoretic treatment.

### 7.4 Structure as compression: a description-length bridge to the volume dynamics

The two bridges above concern $\sigma$ and $\phi$ in isolation and are, as stated, static — they relate a structural mechanism to a coding or mutual-information counterpart without reference to how the corpus grows. There is a third connection, to the algorithmic-information side of the field (Minimum Description Length, Kolmogorov complexity), that is more speculative than the first two but has a compensating virtue: it engages the volume dynamics that are otherwise this framework's departure from information theory, and so it is the bridge most worth flagging even though we develop it least.

The idea is to view structure $S$ as a *generative compression model* of the corpus. Under an MDL reading, the cost of describing a corpus is the description length of the model plus the description length of the data given the model. A schema pays an up-front model cost but shortens the per-datum cost, because the regularities it captures — exactly the enrichment $\phi$, the derivable-rather-than-stored facts — need not be spelled out for each instance, and the states it forbids — exactly the dampening $\sigma$ — need never be described at all. Inconsistency, in this framing, is *description-length overhead*: data that fails to compress under the model, forcing the joint description to bloat because it deviates from every regularity the structure encodes. A contradiction is expensive precisely because it cannot be predicted from the rest of the corpus given the model.

What makes this bridge worth the risk is that description length is not static in the way Shannon channel capacity is. It grows with the corpus, and how it grows is exactly the question this paper asks. An unstructured corpus's description length grows with the accumulating, unconstrained noise — its incompressible remainder rises with the compounding inconsistency of Section 3.2. A well-structured corpus's description length grows predictably, because the model absorbs the regularities and forbids the irregular states, holding the incompressible remainder in check. In MDL terms, the coherence bound of Section 3 is a statement about whether the model (the structure $S$) can be enriched fast enough to keep the corpus's description length growing sub-critically as $V \to \infty$, rather than bloating combinatorially under unconstrained $\epsilon$. This is the same claim the paper makes in its own terms, and the fact that it can be restated in a description-length frame is mild evidence that the volume dynamics are not as far outside information theory as an unqualified reading would suggest — they are outside *Shannon's static* theory, but the algorithmic-information branch, which is natively about description under a model, is a more natural home. We do not develop this into a formal MDL treatment here, and we flag it as speculative; but of the three bridges it is the one that, if pursued, might actually formalize the volume dynamics rather than only the two static factors. The apparatus to do so already exists — the model-selection formalism of Rissanen and Grünwald makes "description length under a model" precise — so the gap here is one of application rather than invention; we mark developing it as a specific future direction (Section 10) rather than attempt it in a working draft.

### 7.5 The honest distance

Even granting the MDL bridge, an honest accounting of the distance remains. The bridges of 7.2 and 7.3 are static, and the MDL connection of 7.4, while it engages growth, is the least developed of the three and rests on an analogy (structure as compression model) we have not made rigorous. The aspect of this framework that remains genuinely underserved by existing theory is the *coupling* of the two exponents into $\beta(V) = 1 + \delta(V) - \gamma(V)$ and the coherence threshold at $V_c$ — the claim that a corpus can cross from a self-sustaining to an unsustainable regime purely by growing. Shannon's static theory has no analogue for this, and while MDL is the right neighborhood to look for one, we have not found it there so much as pointed at where it might be. The flavor is entropic — inconsistency accumulates like disorder and structure is the work done to hold it locally low — but we continue to decline to build a second formal apparatus (thermodynamic or algorithmic) on that observation within this paper. We note the MDL bridge precisely because it narrows the honest distance from "the volume dynamics are outside information theory" to the more accurate "the volume dynamics are outside *Shannon's* information theory, and plausibly reachable from its algorithmic-information branch, though we have not made the reach rigorous here."

### 7.6 Summary

The framework is thus *compatible with* information theory without being *derived from* it. The dampening mechanism has a real and potentially rigorous counterpart in error-correcting redundancy; the enrichment mechanism has a plausible counterpart in accessible mutual information; and the volume dynamics, rather than being wholly orthogonal to information theory as earlier framing suggested, appear reachable in principle from its algorithmic-information (MDL) branch, though we leave that reach undeveloped. We claim the first two bridges to the degree stated and the third only as a promising direction. The value of drawing these connections is not borrowed rigor but two candidate routes toward replacing this paper's illustrative functional forms with principled ones: coding bounds on $\sigma$, and a description-length treatment of the volume dynamics themselves.

## 8. Experimental Program

The framework makes testable predictions. We describe here the experiments we intend to run; results are placeholders pending data collection. We are candid that these are, at present, experimental *designs* rather than fully specified protocols: the predictions below are stated qualitatively (the sign of an exponent, the direction of an effect, the ordering of conditions), and a submission-ready version would need to specify effect sizes, the model-comparison criterion by which a power-law fit would be preferred to alternatives, the corpus sizes required to estimate exponents with usable confidence intervals, and the null model each prediction is tested against. We leave these specifications for the point at which each experiment is run against real data, and flag their current absence rather than gloss it. The experiments are ordered from foundational (operationalizing $\epsilon$, §8.1) through targeted probes of individual claims (§8.2–8.5) to the most direct test of the core thesis, structured agentic memory over task horizon (§8.6), which uniquely supplies a critical volume $V_c$ known in advance — the context window — rather than one recoverable only by fitting. A final experiment (§8.7) re-examines the text-to-SQL semantic layer as a test of the framework's account of denormalization as lost dampening, and is the first to be given a full standalone protocol (RFC-002).

### 8.1 Operationalizing $\epsilon$, and coherence decay under fixed structure

This experiment carries a prior burden the others do not: before decay can be measured, $\epsilon$ must be given a concrete, reproducible definition, per the caveat in Section 3.1.1. We treat this as part of the experiment rather than a precondition settled in advance.

**Prediction.** Under any reasonable operationalization of $\epsilon$ that counts direct pairwise contradiction without propagating it through dependent statements, a corpus grown at fixed structural complexity $S$ will show a coherence ratio $\mathcal{C}(V)$ decaying with order $V^{-\beta}$ for an empirically-determined composite exponent $\beta = 1 + \delta - \gamma$, and increasing the fixed level of $S$ will rescale the curve without changing $\beta$. Because $\beta$ combines a conflict-growth and an enrichment-growth term, this experiment must estimate both $\delta$ (via $\epsilon$'s growth) and $\gamma$ (via $I$'s growth) rather than a single decay exponent, and should report whether the fitted $\beta$ is positive (decay), zero (stable), or negative (improvement with volume).

**Method.** First, fix an operational definition of $\epsilon$. Concretely, we propose evaluating pairs of claims with a frozen, fine-tuned cross-encoder or natural-language-inference (NLI) model, restricted to pairs drawn from within a bounded entity neighborhood rather than across the whole corpus (both to reflect the locality of real conflict and to keep the pairwise evaluation tractable), and defining the unit of $\epsilon$ as the density of contradiction-labeled edges over that local subgraph. Report results under at least one alternative definition (e.g., propagated-cascade counting, or a soft $\epsilon$ that sums NLI contradiction *probabilities* rather than thresholded binary labels, per the hard/soft distinction of Section 3.1.1) to characterize how sensitive the fitted exponents are to the choice. Construct or sample corpora of increasing $V$ within a fixed schema. Operationalize $I$ via a task-based measure of usable signal (retrieval precision, question-answering accuracy). Fit the observed $\mathcal{C}(V)$ to candidate decay orders under each $\epsilon$-definition, recovering $\delta$, $\gamma$, and their composite $\beta$.

### 8.2 Leverage growth and the phase boundary

**Prediction.** Corpora whose structural leverage $\Lambda(S(V))$ grows at least as fast as $V^{\beta}$ — with $\beta = 1 + \delta - \gamma$ formed from the clustering exponent $\delta$ (Section 8.3) and the enrichment exponent $\gamma$ (measured jointly), both for the relevant domain — maintain coherence above the floor; those below the boundary degrade. The prediction is specifically that the *sign* of $\beta$, not the magnitude of $\delta$ alone, sorts maintaining from degrading trajectories.

**Method.** Grow matched corpora under different structural-growth policies (fixed, sublinear, linear, matched-to-$\beta$). Estimate $\Lambda$ empirically from measured $\phi$ and $\sigma$. Test whether the boundary predicted in Section 3.4 separates the maintaining from the degrading trajectories, and whether using the domain-specific composite exponent $\beta = 1+\delta-\gamma$ rather than the worst-case $\beta = 1$ better predicts the observed boundary.

### 8.3 The scaling exponents $\delta$ and $\gamma$, and their trajectories

**Prediction.** Because real conflicts are local rather than uniformly pairwise, the empirical growth order of $\epsilon(V)$ will correspond to $\delta$ substantially below 1 in domains with strong entity or schema clustering, and structure that aligns with the corpus's cluster topology will dampen more efficiently than structure that does not. But — following the densification literature [Leskovec, Kleinberg & Faloutsos 2007] — we further predict that $\delta$ is not constant: as a corpus densifies with growth, cross-cluster linkage rises and the measured $\delta$ drifts upward, so a domain's favorable early-life clustering may erode at scale. The enrichment exponent $\gamma$ should likewise be estimated rather than assumed, since it enters $\beta$ on equal footing.

**Method.** Measure the empirical growth order of both $\epsilon$ and $I$ in natural corpora across domains with varying clustering strength, fitting $\epsilon_0(V) \sim V^{1+\delta}$ and $I(V) \sim V^{\gamma}$ to recover both exponents directly, and — critically — fitting them over successive growth windows rather than once, to detect any trajectory $\delta(V)$, $\gamma(V)$. Compare dampening efficiency of topology-aligned versus topology-agnostic structures at matched $\delta$, and test whether applying community detection (Section 4.2) measurably lowers the effective $\delta$ against the densification-driven tendency for it to rise.

*[PLACEHOLDER — Results 8.3: empirical $\delta$ and $\gamma$ per domain; their trajectories over growth windows; whether $\delta(V)$ rises under densification; whether community detection lowers effective $\delta$; resulting sign and trajectory of $\beta$.]*

### 8.5 The programming-language gradient

**Prediction.** Across comparable projects, the volume threshold at which coherence problems become intolerable is higher for stronger type disciplines, other factors controlled.

**Method.** This is the program's hardest empirical lift, because type discipline is confounded with team size, project maturity, and code quality, none of which can be randomized. We propose exploiting a natural experiment that partially controls for domain: TypeScript-versus-JavaScript codebases, and in particular projects that have undergone a JavaScript-to-TypeScript migration, where the same codebase and often the same team appear on both sides of the type-discipline boundary. Mine an existing large-scale open-source dataset ecosystem (e.g., GitHub-derived corpora) for such pairs and migrations, using lines of code as the proxy for $V$ and defect density normalized by LoC — ideally split into pre- and post-migration windows — as the proxy for coherence failure. The prediction is that the volume at which normalized defect density begins to climb steeply (the empirical analogue of $V_c$) is higher on the stronger-typed side. Confounds should be addressed by preferring within-project migration comparisons over cross-project ones wherever the data permits, and by reporting the sensitivity of any threshold estimate to team-size and project-age covariates rather than claiming to have eliminated them.

### 8.6 Structured memory over task horizon: the most direct test of the thesis

Of the experiments proposed here, this one tests the framework's central claim most directly, because it operationalizes volume as *task horizon* rather than raw corpus size — and horizon is precisely where the compounding of inconsistency bites for an agent. It also supplies an unusually clean, non-arbitrary critical volume: the context window.

**Prediction.** For an agent performing tasks of increasing horizon, coherence is maintained without added structure so long as the relevant memory fits within the context window — the model attends over all of it at once, and interpretive dampening is effectively free, in exactly the sense Section 3 attributes to a human reader holding a short text in mind. Past the point where memory must be externalized and retrieved (the context window as a natural $V_c$), coherence degrades unless structure is added, and the degradation should manifest behaviorally: the agent contradicts its own earlier commitments, acts on superseded information, or loses track of established constraints, specifically at the horizon points where earlier context has scrolled out of the window. The framework predicts, further, that the benefit of memory structure is negligible below $V_c$ and grows with horizon above it, since longer tasks accumulate more volume and therefore more compounding for structure to counteract. This is the coherence threshold of Section 3.4 observed directly in agent behavior, with $V_c$ known in advance rather than only fittable in retrospect — the distinguishing feature that makes this experiment sharper than the corpus-based ones above.

**Method.** Hold the agent and task distribution fixed and vary the memory architecture along a ladder that mirrors the taxonomy of Sections 4 and 6: (i) unstructured append-only transcript or log ($\sigma \approx 1$, the prose-equivalent baseline); (ii) embedding-based retrieval over memory (enrichment-dominant, the RAG baseline of Section 4.1); (iii) schema-structured memory with typed entities and relations (compound, per Section 4.1); and (iv) graph memory organized by the a posteriori mechanisms of Sections 4.2–4.3 (community and motif structure). Sweep task horizon across and well beyond the context-window boundary. The prediction is a monotone improvement up the ladder that is negligible below $V_c$ and widens with horizon above it.

Two design requirements are essential rather than optional. First, **measure coherence behaviorally rather than by counting contradictions in the store**, which sidesteps the $\epsilon$-operationalization burden of Section 8.1: track self-contradiction rate (does the agent assert something incompatible with an earlier commitment?), superseded-belief actions (does it act on information a later observation revised?), and constraint retention over horizon (does it still honor a constraint established many steps back?). These are observable proxies for $\epsilon$ that do not require solving the counting problem first. Second, and most importantly, **control for the retrieval-quality confound.** A graph-memory agent may outperform a flat-memory agent simply because its retrieval surfaces better context, not because coherence-maintenance is the operative mechanism — and the paper already concedes in Section 4.1 that graph-augmented retrieval retrieves well. To isolate the coherence effect from the retrieval effect, match retrieval budget across conditions and, critically, report self-consistency measures (which structure should improve specifically via dampening) *separately* from task-success measures (which better retrieval alone could explain). A result showing improved task success but flat self-consistency would support the retrieval story, not the coherence story; the framework specifically predicts that self-consistency improves up the structure ladder, above $V_c$, at matched retrieval budget. An ablation separating dampening-only memory structure (e.g., contradiction-flagged recall) from enrichment-only (e.g., relationship-linked recall) from compound would further test the Section 4.1 claim that the compound quadrant outperforms either single mode rather than merely matching the better of them.

### 8.7 The semantic layer over a denormalized schema (text-to-SQL)

A denormalized relational schema is, in this framework's terms, a corpus that has deliberately traded dampening for read performance: the same domain fact is spread across wide tables, duplicated, and stripped of the relational constraints that made the normalized model self-consistent. Denormalization is a deliberate lowering of $\sigma$ at the physical layer — the coherence the normalized model enforced by construction is gone, and the physical column names and join paths no longer carry the domain's meaning. This is the projection of §4.2.1 in a database rather than an agent's memory: a dimension along which the data was coherent (the normalized domain model) has been flattened away, and what remains reads as locally arbitrary. The framework predicts this is exactly why natural-language-to-SQL degrades on heavily denormalized warehouses — the model is asked to reconstruct load-bearing domain structure the schema has discarded.

A *semantic layer* over such a schema — a metrics, entity, or modeling layer of the kind provided by contemporary analytics tooling — is a priori compound structure sitting at the seam between the query and the physical model. It dampens, by declaring the canonical entities, measures, and relationships the denormalized physical model lost, restoring the domain coherence; and it enriches, by mapping a natural-language query onto derivable join paths and computed measures explicit in no single physical table. It is also the composition/seam mechanism of the Future Work section appearing as a concrete artifact: structure at the interface between intent and store, restoring coherence lost in the projection to physical.

**Prediction.** The benefit of a semantic layer to text-to-SQL accuracy scales with the *degree of denormalization* of the underlying schema — the amount of $\sigma$ the physical model shed — because that degree is precisely how much domain coherence there is to restore. A fully normalized schema should benefit little, its domain coherence still present in the physical model; a heavily denormalized warehouse should benefit most. The framework predicts a monotonic dose-response between denormalization degree and accuracy lift, not merely that semantic layers help on average.

**The key control.** It is tempting to attribute a semantic layer's benefit to better *names and descriptions* (a documentation effect) as opposed to restored *structure* — but naming is itself structural, and this is the sharper version of the control. Naming carries structure at escalating levels: *appropriate* names relieve interpretation, *consistent* names dampen (the same concept given one token, not several — the controlled-vocabulary mechanism applied to identifiers), and *conventional* names enrich (a held convention like `a_id` for table `A`'s key makes join paths inferable from identifiers alone). The control is therefore not naming-versus-structure but a ladder of increasingly structural naming, then explicit declaration on top, with each rung adding one mechanism. The framework predicts that the *dampening* and *enrichment* rungs (consistency and convention) each add accuracy and scale with denormalization degree, while the *interpretation* rung (appropriate naming) adds accuracy without scaling — separating restored derivable structure from mere interpretation relief, while recognizing both as structure.

This experiment is specified in full — operational definitions, corpus and schema selection, the denormalization-degree metric, baselines, and the ladder of structural naming — in the accompanying **RFC-002**, and its results will be reported there against the prediction stated here.

## 9. Limitations

We reiterate and extend the caveats stated at the outset.

**The formalism is illustrative, its exponents may not be constants, and one of its quantities is not yet well-defined.** The specific functional forms for $\phi$, $\sigma$, and the two scaling exponents — the conflict-growth exponent $\delta$ and the enrichment exponent $\gamma$ — are chosen for tractability and intuition, not derived from first principles or fitted to data. We have moreover treated $\delta$ and $\gamma$ as constants while arguing in Section 3.4 that at least $\delta$ likely drifts with scale under network densification; if both exponents move with $V$, the composite $\beta = 1 + \delta - \gamma$ is itself a trajectory rather than a number, and the clean regime boundaries ($\beta \lessgtr 0$) become moving targets a system can cross in either direction as it grows. More seriously still, $\epsilon(V)$ lacks a settled operational definition: Section 3.1.1 flags both that cascading inconsistency has no obviously correct unit and that hard (logical) and soft (probabilistic) inconsistency are distinct phenomena that need not scale together, so different reasonable choices could yield different fitted values of $\delta$ — and even different *signs* of $\beta$ — in Section 8. The framework's contribution is the decomposition and the qualitative shape of the bound, in particular the identification of $\beta$'s sign as the governing question, not the exact curves, the constancy of the exponents, or the exact unit of $\epsilon$.

**Dampening has a write-time cost the model does not price.** Section 5.1 makes explicit what the main line of argument otherwise elides: enforcing $\sigma(S)$ is not free at the point of writing new data, and in distributed systems it competes directly with write throughput. The bound in Section 3.4 should be read as applying to *effective*, enforced dampening, which in practice is often lower than the nominal dampening a schema declares, because systems relax enforcement under write pressure.

**Coherence is defined narrowly.** Absence of contradiction is necessary but not sufficient for information quality; relevance, completeness, and freshness are out of scope and are not captured by $\mathcal{C}$.

**Structure is not free, and can be wrong.** The model treats structure as beneficial, but schema design has cost, and structure built on mistaken assumptions can propagate systematic error — a badly structured corpus may be worse than an unstructured one. The freshness cost of materialized structure (Section 5) is one facet of this; mis-specification is another.

**Graphs are one option among several, and the compound claim requires an enforced type.** The argument motivates *appropriate compound structure*, not graphs uniquely, and Sections 4 and 6 have already qualified the graph case directly: a bare property graph's edges are generative but not, by themselves, dampening. The compounding this paper attributes to graphs holds for a graph governed by a *declared, write-time-enforced graph type* (in the ISO-GQL sense, as implemented in Cypher), not for unconstrained instance data — and not for a graph subjected only to after-the-fact shape *validation*, SHACL or ShEx used to report nonconformance retrospectively, which is a posteriori inspection. The distinction is one of enforcement rather than language: the same shape language wired as a write-gate enforces a priori, though with a weaker, bypassable guarantee than an engine-enforced ISO-GQL graph type (Sections 4, 5.1). A reader should not take this paper to claim that adopting a graph model alone secures the coherence benefits described; the declared type is doing indispensable work that the instance layer does not do on its own, and that enforcement carries the write-time coordination cost discussed in Section 5.1. Vector spaces with learned metrics, probabilistic graphical models, and formal ontologies also occupy the compound quadrant once comparably constrained, so the case for graphs specifically rests on convenience and the naturalness of expressing both type and instance in one typed vocabulary, not on graphs being formally unique.

**The error-correction bridge addresses structural noise, not semantic drift.** As Section 7.2 claims it, the analogy between dampening and error-correcting redundancy is structural: a schema, like a code, adds redundancy that renders violations detectable. But channel coding classically addresses random corruption of an otherwise-fixed message, whereas real corpus incoherence is substantially driven by *compounding semantic divergence* — evolving business logic, conflicting human definitions, or temporal drift in what a term means. The structural analogy holds regardless of why a violation arose, but a causal story in which structure "corrects errors" the way a code corrects bit-flips would overstate the bridge; structure detects and rejects violations, but it does not by itself resolve the semantic disagreements that produced them.

**The coherence threshold is a sign change, not a physical phase transition.** The boundary of Section 3.4 is the point where an exponent changes sign; real degradation around it is likely gradual and domain-dependent rather than sharp and universal. We have renamed it from "phase transition" to "coherence threshold" (Section 3.4.2) precisely to avoid claiming the non-analytic signatures a physical phase transition would carry. The construction is still useful — $V_c$ gives the threshold a datable, testable form — but whether real systems exhibit anything resembling a sharp crossing at $V_c$ or merely a gradual steepening is itself an empirical question.

**Engagement with prior art is currently partial.** This draft positions itself against a deliberately small set of references and leaves several directly relevant literatures under-engaged. The most consequential gap is the formal literature on *inconsistency measurement* in knowledge bases — minimal inconsistent subsets, paraconsistent reasoning, graded and probabilistic inconsistency scores — which bears directly on the paper's least-settled quantity, $\epsilon$, and against which the hard/soft distinction of Section 3.1.1 should be positioned rather than presented as if it were the natural taxonomy. Related gaps include the knowledge-graph data-quality literature (which already has dimensions for consistency, accuracy, completeness, and currency that the coherence ratio touches), the machine-learning scaling-laws literature (Section 3.5.1's functional-form question), and the classical normalization-theory account of update anomalies that Section 6 invokes without citing. We flag these as work required before submission rather than pretending the present reference set is adequate; a framework claiming cross-disciplinary synthesis owes a fuller account of what it synthesizes.

## 10. Future Work

Beyond the experimental program of Section 8, several directions would extend the framework rather than test it. The first is substantially larger than the others and marks, we think, the path from the present work to a theory of knowledge work proper.

**From coherence maintenance to coherence under composition.** This paper deliberately treats the *source* of statements as exogenous: volume $V$ arrives from outside, and the framework describes how structure must scale to keep the accumulating corpus coherent. This makes the present work a theory of a *reservoir* — how a growing store of statements retains coherence — and not yet a theory of knowledge *work*, which is centrally about the *generation* of new statements from existing ones, whether by human authorship or by autoregressive models. Endogenizing generation is the natural sequel, and it is best approached not through the narrow case of a single feedback loop but through composition in general.

The narrow case is worth stating to see why it is too narrow. When a generator reads from the same corpus it writes to — an agent whose outputs become its future memory, a model whose training data includes model outputs — the source becomes self-referential: $V$ is now partly a function of the corpus's own prior state passed through a generator, which can inject inconsistency the world never contributed (hallucination as endogenous $\epsilon$) or, if steered by structure, can generate more coherently. This closed loop is real and important, but it is only one topology. Generation also composes in pipelines of distinct transformations (summarize, then translate, then extract), in fan-out from one source to many consumers, in fan-in merging many sources, and in chains of distinct agents each transforming the last one's output. The general object is the *composition of coherence-affecting operations in arbitrary arrangement*, of which the self-referential loop is merely the reflexive special case, where the chain closes on itself.

The degenerate, structure-free case of composition is the childhood game of Chinese whispers, and it isolates the phenomenon precisely. Each participant hears, interprets, and re-emits, and the message degrades — not through random noise but through the compounding of inconsistency this paper models, now occurring *across composition boundaries* rather than within an accumulating store. What makes it degrade is that nothing structural passes between steps: only prose, which each step is free to reinterpret. The seam between one transformation and the next carries $\sigma \approx 1$ — no dampening whatsoever. And this is exactly what licenses the generalization: play the same game with a *structured* artifact passing between steps — a filled schema, a graph fragment, a typed record — and each step is constrained to read and emit against a shared structure, dampening the loss at every seam. The telephone game played with structured messages does not drift the way it does with prose. This is the paper's thesis relocated from the reservoir to the seam: structure's role in composition is to *survive the interfaces between operations*, which is a genuinely different function from dampening conflict within a single store — it is dampening *transmission loss across a boundary* — even though it maps onto the same $\sigma$.

This reframing points at a theorem-shaped question the present paper does not attempt: *under what conditions is coherence preserved under composition?* Composing operations $f \circ g \circ h$, each with its own enrichment and dampening at its interfaces, coherence might degrade multiplicatively — the structure-free Chinese-whispers regime, where each seam loses a fixed fraction and the product decays geometrically in the length of the chain — or sufficient interface structure might render the composition coherence-preserving, a fixed point that survives arbitrarily long chains. Here composition *length* plays the role that volume $V$ plays in the present paper: the same compounding of inconsistency, now indexed by how many transformations deep a pipeline runs, with structure at the seams as the counterforce, and with a critical length analogous to the critical volume $V_c$ of Section 3.4 beyond which an unstructured pipeline's coherence collapses. This is the practical content behind the familiar observation that long agent chains, multi-hop tool pipelines, and multi-agent handoffs drift: they are Chinese whispers with insufficient structure at the seams. A theory of knowledge work, on this view, is a theory of coherence under composition, with the endogenous generative loop recovered as the reflexive case and the present paper's single-store bound recovered as the length-one case. We regard establishing the statics of the single store, as this paper attempts, as the necessary groundwork for that dynamics.

**From internal coherence to external and intersubjective coherence.** Section 1.1 defined coherence narrowly, as the absence of internal contradiction, and disclaimed any account of information *quality* — a corpus can be perfectly coherent and entirely false, internally consistent fiction scoring maximally on $\mathcal{C}$. That disclaimer marks a boundary the framework touches without crossing, and the crossing is a natural, if philosophically weightier, extension. Internal coherence relates to two things the present paper sets aside: *correspondence*, whether a corpus agrees with observable reality, and *consensus*, whether the corpora of distinct actors agree with one another. Both are recognizably the composition problem above, specialized: consensus composes whole belief-holding corpora across the seams that are agreements between actors, and dampening at those seams is exactly what a shared schema, a common vocabulary, or an agreed ontology provides — which is why standards, controlled vocabularies, and shared ontologies exist at all, as consensus infrastructure that pre-dampens the boundaries between actors who will later need to agree. The paper's a priori/a posteriori distinction carries over directly: a shared schema is pre-agreed interface structure, while an emergent common vocabulary detected across actors after the fact is its a posteriori counterpart.

Correspondence is the harder half, and structure plays a specific and perhaps surprising role in it: structure is what makes a corpus *falsifiable*. Unstructured prose can absorb a contradicting observation by quiet reinterpretation; a typed, constrained corpus forces the contradiction into the open, where reality can reject it. On this view structure raises a corpus's surface area of contact with the world — it is what lets observation push back — and the same $\sigma$ that suppresses internal conflict also *exposes* conflict with external evidence so it cannot be papered over. The scientific method is arguably this mechanism institutionalized: shared formalism, measurement protocol, and agreed units as the structure that makes disagreement with reality legible enough to compel belief revision. And the two halves turn out to be coupled — the structure that makes one actor's corpus correspond to reality is largely the same structure that makes it agree with another's, because reality is the shared referent every corpus is attempting to describe. Agreement across subjects and agreement with the object are the same act of structural reconciliation seen twice.

We flag this direction precisely because it is the one that would connect the framework to truth, and precisely because it is philosophically loaded in a way the rest of the paper is not — it touches correspondence, realism, and the sociology of knowledge, each an older and harder problem than the one addressed here. We name the extension and the one structural insight we think it turns on — structure as the mechanism of falsifiability, and so of both correction by reality and agreement among actors — and deliberately decline to resolve the epistemology. The present framework describes internal coherence; whether and how internal coherence is made to track external truth is a question it can point at but does not pretend to answer.

**Graph grammars as induced, prescriptive motif structure.** Section 4.3 treats motif detection as strictly a posteriori and diagnostic: it discovers recurring configurations and flags deviations, but does not forbid them, which is what keeps it on the shape-based rather than type-based side of Section 4.1's distinction. A natural next step is to close the loop between the two sides. Once motifs have been detected a posteriori, the recurring ones could be lifted into an explicit **graph grammar** — a set of production rules stating which local configurations are permissible — which would then be enforced *prescriptively* at write time, exactly as a type system's grammar validates syntax. This is a genuinely hybrid mechanism: a posteriori discovery feeding a priori enforcement, converting a detected regularity into a declared constraint. We deliberately do not fold it into Section 4.3, because doing so would blur the a priori/a posteriori distinction that section depends on; it is better understood as a distinct, downstream construction that consumes the output of a posteriori detection and re-enters the a priori regime with it. Formalizing when such lifting is warranted — which detected motifs are stable and load-bearing enough to be worth promoting to enforced grammar rules, and at what freshness and write-latency cost per Section 5 — is a well-posed problem this framework motivates but does not solve.

**A description-length formalization of the volume dynamics.** Section 7.4 sketched, without developing, an MDL reading in which structure is a generative compression model and inconsistency is description-length overhead. Making this rigorous — deriving the coherence bound of Section 3 as a statement about sub-critical growth of description length under a model — would be the most direct route to replacing the paper's illustrative exponents with principled ones, and would substantiate the claim that the volume dynamics are reachable from algorithmic information theory rather than wholly outside it.

**Coding bounds on $\sigma$.** As flagged in Section 7.2, the error-correcting-redundancy bridge poses a well-defined question — whether there is a bound on how much inconsistency a given quantity of structural redundancy can suppress — whose answer would supply $\sigma(S)$ with a principled functional form.

**Empirical estimation of $\delta(V)$ and $\gamma(V)$ trajectories and the critical volume $V_c$.** The densification literature predicts $\delta(V)$ rises with scale; measuring these trajectories in real corpora, and testing whether the critical-volume estimate $V_c$ actually predicts observed coherence breakdown, would move the coherence-threshold construction from a formal possibility to a validated forecasting tool.

## 11. Conclusion

We have described what appears to be a latent feature of any growing body of information with relational regularity: that its coherence, the ratio of the signal it carries to the inconsistency it accumulates, is governed by how much structure it already embodies relative to its volume — and that this governance follows a regularity we can state, at least provisionally, as a race between two exponents: how fast inconsistency compounds and how fast usable signal grows. Whether more data must be paid for with more structure, costs nothing, or actively helps turns on the sign of their difference, not on scale alone. We did not impose this relationship on the corpora we discuss; we have tried to describe one already at work in them, in the same spirit that a law of motion describes bodies that were already moving. By decoupling enrichment from dampening we obtain a vocabulary for distinguishing kinds of structure, from the dampening-only controlled vocabulary to the enrichment-only embedding to the compound type system and typed graph — a compounding that, we have been careful to note, belongs to a graph governed by a declared, write-time-enforced type rather than to unconstrained instance data on its own, and that carries a write-time coordination cost the materialization framing alone does not price. Placing representations on a continuum from prose to graphs, with the type disciplines of programming languages as a running parallel, we find that coherence is reached not by piling on structure but by making explicit the structure whose enrichment and dampening compound — at a cost the practitioner can and should weigh against how their domain's two exponents actually behave, including the possibility, under network densification, that the balance shifts against them as the corpus grows. We offer the framework as a lens for information-architecture decisions and have set out the experiments — including the prior task of giving inconsistency itself a workable unit of measure — that would test its predictions. We have also been explicit about its boundary: the present work is a *statics*, treating the source of statements as exogenous and characterizing the standing relationship between structure, volume, and coherence in a single store. The corresponding *dynamics* — coherence under the composition of coherence-affecting operations, with generation endogenized and the self-referential agent loop as its reflexive case — is the path from this theory of coherence maintenance to a theory of knowledge work, and we have sketched it as future work rather than claimed it here. Whether the specific curves hold is an empirical question, and Section 8 sets out how to answer it; the reframing — that every architecture decision is a bet on where reasoning work should live, trading write-time cost for the read-time optimization that structure earns back — stands independent of the outcome.

A reader is entitled to weigh this when assessing whether the taxonomy is descriptively neutral, and several of the paper's design choices (the selection of motivating examples, the ordering of the prose-to-graph continuum, the positioning of vector embeddings as enrichment without dampening) do consistently favor the graph endpoint. We note two internal checks that were made specifically to guard against this pull rather than to indulge it: the paper argues at length that a *bare* property graph is only enrichment-dominant and earns the compound classification solely once governed by a declared, write-time-enforced graph type (Sections 4.1, 4.2, 6), and it states plainly that vector spaces with learned metrics, probabilistic graphical models, and formal ontologies occupy the same compound quadrant once comparably constrained, so the case for graphs specifically rests on convenience and expressive naturalness rather than on formal uniqueness (Section 9). These checks do not neutralize the conflict; they are offered so the reader can see where it was actively resisted and judge the remainder accordingly.
